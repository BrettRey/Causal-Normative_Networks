# Revision hook: Rajagopalan on agentic-AI personhood

**Status:** note only; don't alter the Synthese manuscript while it is under review.

## Source

Shruti Rajagopalan, “Governing Agentic AI: Why Legal Personhood Is Neither Necessary nor Sufficient,” working-paper revision, 15 July 2026. Local text: `/Users/brettreynolds/Downloads/ssrn-7127038.md`.

## Fit

Rajagopalan supplies a clean application of the paper's assessment separations. Legal personality may be validly conferred while remaining weak as an enforcement device. Her registration, identification, verification, financial-responsibility, lifecycle-traceability, and suspension stack may improve causal uptake, recovery, deterrence, and cessation without settling moral warrant.

This supports a revision-stage distinction among:

- **legal status:** whether the system is constituted as a legal person;
- **social and institutional efficacy:** whether actors recognize and act on that status;
- **enforceability:** whether a claimant or regulator can locate a bearer, recover, and stop the activity;
- **moral warrant:** whether the status assignment or resulting treatment is justified.

The paper should resist Rajagopalan's sentence that the stack does the work for which personhood was “only ever a stand-in.” Success on the enforcement projection doesn't establish that personhood has no moral, recognitional, claimant-side, expressive, or rights-conferring purposes. That move is an instance of the very assessment collapse this paper diagnoses.

## Possible use after a revise-and-resubmit decision

A compact application could follow the discussion of institutional status and efficacy: legal status can be effective yet poorly enforceable, while an activity-based regime can improve enforceability without conferring status or establishing warrant. Zhang, Koyejo, and Yang's context-instability paper adds no material argument here and shouldn't be imported.
