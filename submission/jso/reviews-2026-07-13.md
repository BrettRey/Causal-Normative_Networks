## Reviewer A (verbatim)

> Unfortunately, I must recommend rejection without resubmission.
>
> The main problems with the manuscript are structural.
>
> The first concerns the paper's central research question. What exactly is the problem the author is trying to solve? I understand that the author aims to extend Khalidi's account of projectible kinds to the social domain, but it remains unclear what problems this extension is intended to address. Which contemporary debate in social ontology does the paper contribute to? I am not suggesting that the paper fails to address an important problem or that it makes no contribution. Rather, the author does not clearly identify either the problem motivating the paper or the debate to which it contributes.
>
> The second problem concerns the paper's argument. It is often difficult, if not impossible, to follow the author's line of reasoning. For example, the author frequently refers in the main text to sections by number, yet the sections themselves are not numbered, making these references impossible to follow.
>
> The third problem is semantic. Technical concepts such as "social cascade" and "anchoring" are introduced without clear definitions. Instead, the author appears to assume that the reader is already familiar with these concepts. As a result, the paper is unnecessarily difficult to follow.
>
> Recommendation: Decline Submission

## Reviewer B (verbatim)

> My main concern is that the paper substitutes terminology for analysis. Many of its central notions are introduced through suggestive labels rather than explicit definitions, and their role in the overall argument remains difficult to determine. Expressions such as life-cycle, profile, projection, and causal-normative network create an appearance of theoretical depth, but it is often unclear whether they identify genuine explanatory relations. This lack of conceptual precision is particularly problematic because the paper's central claim depends on extending Khalidi's notion of projectibility to normative and social cases. However, the paper's reliance on Khalidi's notion of projectibility is insufficiently motivated. In Khalidi's account, projectibility is introduced to explain the inferential and explanatory significance of natural kinds within causal networks. Here, however, the focus is on speech acts, obligations, institutional statuses, and other normative phenomena. It is never made sufficiently clear in what sense such cases are supposed to be projectible, why they should be treated as analogous to the cases that motivate Khalidi's framework. The central phenomenon remains inadequately specified, and the proposed account remains too unclear for its contribution to be properly assessed. For these reasons, I do not believe the paper is suitable for publication in its present form.
>
> Recommendation: Decline Submission

## Actionable punch list (from the reports)

1. **State the problem and the debate.** Both reviewers: name the contemporary social-ontology debate the paper enters and the problem the Khalidi extension solves, up front.
2. **Number the sections.** Reviewer A: in-text references to numbered sections but the sections aren't numbered — a build/formatting bug that made the argument un-followable. Fix immediately regardless of venue.
3. **Define the technical vocabulary.** social cascade, anchoring (Rev A); life-cycle, profile, projection, causal-normative network (Rev B). Explicit definitions, not assumed familiarity.
4. **Motivate the projectibility transfer.** Reviewer B's core: Khalidi's projectibility explains inference/explanation for natural kinds in causal networks; make explicit in what sense speech acts / obligations / institutional statuses are projectible, and why they are analogous. This is the load-bearing argument and it is currently missing.
5. **Show the apparatus does explanatory work.** Reviewer B: the terms must identify genuine explanatory relations, not decorate. For each (life-cycle, profile, projection, causal-normative network), show what it explains that a plainer description would not.
