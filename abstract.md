# Abstract (draft)

Some social and moral explanations need both causal relations and normative transitions in one structure, without reducing either to the other. This paper asks what such a structure is. A causal-normative network is a typed, heterogeneous explanatory structure: normative statuses alter practical positions, causal mechanisms transmit uptake and response, and recognition links let agents and institutions track, mis-track, or constitutively alter statuses. Such networks are neither causal graphs with moral labels added nor normative taxonomies with sociology appended. Building on Khalidi (2018) on ordered causal networks and Boyd (1999) on homeostatic clusters, but going beyond both, I argue that four kinds of dependence (causal, constitutive, normative-transition, and recognitional) compose one network without collapsing into one relation-type. The coupling is counterfactually real: status-rule interventions, recognition interventions, and authorized-recognition interventions come apart. Misrecognition is the diagnostic case; it explains how an invalid status-assignment becomes socially effective without becoming morally valid.

Keywords: natural kinds, projectibility, normativity, speech acts, deontic status, social ontology, causal networks, recognition, misrecognition.
