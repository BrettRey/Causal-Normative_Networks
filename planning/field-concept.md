# Field Concept

Date: 2026-05-25; revised 2026-05-26

Purpose: record the literature-grounded account of `field` for the causal-normative networks paper.

## Working Decision

Use `field` for an ordinary normative practice or domain that parameterizes the causal-normative network: contract law, criminal law, medical ethics, ordinary promising, friendship, workplace discipline, academic authorship. A field is not a further node in the graph. It fixes which performances count, which statuses are available, which conditions generate or defeat them, which recognitions are authoritative, which records matter, and which responses are licensed.

The reader-facing formulation should be simple:

> A field is the practice relative to which a status supports projections.

The technical support stays in the background. Epstein explains frame principles and anchors; Boyd explains why projectibility is practice-relative; Searle and Ásta distinguish institutional and communal status-conferral. Do not make `field` a homeostatic property cluster, and do not make "anchored parameter package" the front-door definition. That phrase is useful as backstage infrastructure, not as the paper's main wording.

## Post-Review-Board Adjustment

The 2026-05-26 review-board pass pushed one further clarification: do not use `field-validity` as a blanket label for every social status. Use `field-validity` mainly for institutional or rule-governed fields, where office, procedure, record, and rule can be assessed directly. Use `successful conferral` for communal cases, where the question is whether agents with standing in context have imposed real constraints and enablements. Both can be socially effective without moral warrant.

The network should be described as an explanatory representation. It shows statuses, correlative positions, recognition, and uptake together; it does not replace the grounding story. Epstein supplies the grounding/anchoring distinction, while the graph shows how grounded statuses become projectible in a practice.

The machinery pass completed after that review-board pass adds two compact devices to the manuscript. Table 1 is a projection ledger for promise, consent, judgment, and discrimination: field, source item, main edge, projection, defeater or limit. Table 2 separates social efficacy, institutional field-validity, communal successful conferral, recognitional fit, enforceability/finality, and moral warrant. This is the cleanest answer to the validity question: validity is not one thing, and the table makes the separations inspectable.

Judgment is now the institutional anchor case. The added ALI Judgments source supports cautious claims about adjudicated status, records, finality/preclusion, and remedial or enforcement profiles. Keep legal detail modest unless the official volume is consulted.

## Field-Validity

A status-assignment is field-valid when it satisfies the status-generating conditions fixed by the relevant field. In an institutional field, that often means the right office, procedure, form, record, or rule. In a communal field, the stricter and clearer formulation is `successful conferral`: a socially salient base or ascription is taken up by agents with standing in that context and confers real constraints and enablements.

Field-validity is not the same as social efficacy. A valid contract can be missed by a particular audience. A release can terminate an obligation even if the promisee keeps demanding performance. A judgment can create a procedural/remedial position even if people grumble about it.

Field-validity is also not moral warrant. A field can have defective rules, unjust offices, or oppressive standing relations. In those cases the assignment can be field-valid, or successfully conferred, and socially effective while lacking moral warrant.

## Boundaries and Contestation

Fields can be fuzzy, overlapping, and contested. That is not a defect in the account. It explains why the same act can be an interpersonal promise but not a contract, medical consent but not sexual consent, a legally operative judgment but not a morally warranted finding.

Counter-practices should be treated as rival or partial practices, not as simple failures of recognition. This is important for oppressive cases: resistant uptake can coexist with official, professional, workplace, or communal uptake.

## What We Do Not Need Yet

No new source is required to stabilize the field concept. Possible additions if a referee asks:

- Guala on institutions as rules-in-equilibrium.
- Hindriks on institutional facts, functions, or collective agency.
- Ritchie on social structures and organized groups.
- Bourdieu only if the paper chooses to engage the technical sociology of fields, which it currently should not.
- Schatzki/practice theory only if the paper shifts toward a general theory of practices.

Those would broaden the project. The current paper needs a compact infrastructure concept, not a full theory of fields.
