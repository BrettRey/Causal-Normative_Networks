# Causal-Normative Networks: Design Brief

Provenance: Brett's specification, 2026-05-24. This is the foundational companion to the act-kinds paper (`papers/Moral_act-kinds_as_nodes_in_causal-normative_networks/`).

## Why this paper

The act-kinds paper uses the causal-normative network idea to explain the projectibility of moral act-kinds. This paper makes *causal-normative network* itself the explanandum. The act-kinds paper's Section 3 already asserts, in compressed form, that the network is not merely causal, that deontic statuses are not hidden causal mechanisms, and that the structure includes statuses, transitions, uptake, and response profiles. It distinguishes constitutive status links, normative transition links, causal uptake links, and recognition/misrecognition links. Each compressed claim invites a metaphysical objection. This paper takes those objections as its main business.

## Two questions

- Act-kinds paper: why are moral act-kinds (promising, consent, coercion, betrayal) projectible? Answer: because they locate acts in causal-normative transition networks.
- This paper: what kind of explanatory structure contains both causal relations and normative transitions without reducing either to the other? Answer: a typed, coupled structure.

## Thesis

A causal-normative network is a typed, heterogeneous explanatory structure in which normative statuses alter practical positions, causal mechanisms transmit uptake and response, and recognition links connect the two by allowing agents and institutions to track, mis-track, or constitutively alter statuses. Such networks are neither ordinary causal graphs with moral labels added nor normative taxonomies with sociology appended. They are coupled structures whose explanatory power depends on the interaction of constitutive, normative, causal, and recognitional relations.

## The gap (why it is worth writing)

- Khalidi (2018) gives the ordered causal-network model for kinds: projectibility grounded in ordered causal properties and relations, not mere concatenation of properties. He does not give the mixed-edge structure.
- Sbisà (2023) gives a bridge from speech-act theory to deontic effects: illocutionary types can be characterized by assignment or cancellation of deontic-modal predicates. She does not give a general account of a network whose edges are partly causal, partly constitutive, partly normative, and partly recognitional.

This paper should be Khalidi-forward. Boyd can remain available as compact background on homeostatic property clusters and anti-essentialist projectibility, but he should not carry the main scaffold. The live gap is that Khalidi gives ordered causal networks, while the moral and institutional cases require a mixed-edge extension of network order. Sbisà supplies a speech-act route into deontic effects, but not the general typed network.

## Four kinds of dependence (the core distinction)

1. Causal dependence. One event, disposition, institutional procedure, affective response, record, sanction, or expectation helps produce another. (A public promise causes reliance; a legal judgment causes enforcement; a record of consent causes later institutional treatment.)
2. Constitutive dependence. One status partly consists in, or necessarily brings with it, another position internal to the practice. (A directed obligation gives the promisee standing to demand performance; that standing is part of being owed something, not a predictable effect of the obligation.)
3. Normative-transition dependence. One status changes into, generates, blocks, terminates, defeats, or modifies another. (Release terminates an obligation; withdrawal terminates a permission; breach generates a secondary profile of demand, blame, apology, repair, or excuse.)
4. Recognition dependence. Agents or institutions track, fail to track, or mis-track a status. Sometimes recognition merely registers a status; sometimes authorized recognition creates or alters it. (A court judgment may create enforceable liability; a bystander's belief that someone has been released does not create release.)

The act-kinds paper already contains this apparatus in compressed form. This paper slows down and argues that these relations can compose one network without collapsing into one relation-type.

## The central opponent

Not essentialism (that is the act-kinds paper's opponent). The opponent here is the forced-choice view:

- either the network is genuinely causal, in which case normative statuses must reduce to beliefs, sanctions, incentives, dispositions, or institutional behaviour;
- or the network is genuinely normative, in which case causal language is only a metaphor for rules, standings, entailments, and classifications.

Reply: no. Some social-moral explanations require mixed networks. The obligation is not a causal mechanism, but a practice containing obligations has causal organization, because agents and institutions recognize, rely on, enforce, misrecognize, resent, excuse, record, and repair in patterned ways. The network is explanatory because the normative and causal relations are coupled. Naming and defending that coupling is the job.

## Structure (working, 8 sections)

1. The problem of mixed explanation. Cases where neither causal nor normative explanation is enough on its own (promise, consent). Target = normative alteration plus causal uptake. Introduce the four dependence types.
2. Why ordinary causal networks are not enough. Engage Khalidi. Pivot sentence: "Khalidi is right that projectibility requires order; the moral and institutional cases show that not all order is causal order."
3. Why normative taxonomies are not enough. A taxonomy without uptake, recognition, misrecognition, enforcement, reliance, and repair cannot explain projectibility. Use the act-kinds paper's promise-breaking life-cycle as evidence, then abstract: normative position plus causal uptake is the general explanatory unit.
4. The typed-edge model. Nodes and edges (inventory below). The heterogeneity is the point, not a defect.
5. Coupling and intervention. Coupled counterfactual structure. Three intervention types: status-rule, recognition, authorized-recognition.
6. Misrecognition as the diagnostic case. Defeats both reductions; handles oppression by separating social efficacy from moral validity.
7. Fields. A field fixes which edges are live, which agents have authority, which recognitions are status-altering, which records count, and which projections matter. Fields parameterize a shared typed network (avoids both universalism and relativism).
8. Conclusion. The strongest claim.

### Typed-edge inventory

Nodes: performances (utterances, signatures, bodily actions, omissions); deontic statuses (obligation, permission, power, liability, immunity, standing); transition events (undertaking, authorization, release, withdrawal, breach, defeat); recognition states (belief, record, official classification, institutional uptake); response states (blame, resentment, apology, repair, sanction, trust update); field structures (legal, interpersonal, institutional, professional, medical, epistemic).

Edges: causal; constitutive; normative-transition; recognitional; authority-conferring; projective (classification licenses defeasible inferences about later responses).

## Coupling and intervention (detail)

- Status-rule interventions alter the normative layer and then propagate through causal uptake.
- Recognition interventions alter causal uptake and may or may not alter status.
- Authorized-recognition interventions alter both.

Worked contrasts: lacking authority, the same words may fail to bind; after release, the same non-performance is no longer breach; after withdrawal, the same physical act is unauthorized; a person falsely believed to have promised faces demands they do not owe; a court judgment, registration, accepted resignation, or formal release can alter the status it records.

## Misrecognition (detail) and the oppression payoff

Misrecognition can be causally powerful while normatively defective: it can expose a person to demands, sanctions, mistrust, exclusion, and institutional consequences they do not owe or deserve. Discriminatory classification creates socially effective positions without creating valid degraded standing. The apparatus explains how an invalid status-assignment becomes socially real in its effects without becoming morally valid. This is likely the most important section.

## Fields (detail)

Interpersonal morality: breach projects toward blame, apology, repair, trust revision. Contract law: breach projects toward liability, enforceability, damages, or excuse. Medicine: consent projects toward authorization, protocol, liability, trust. Speech-act practice: an illocutionary act alters the deontic-modal position of participants (close to Sbisà). Fields do not create arbitrary projections; they parameterize a shared typed network.

## The strongest version

Avoid the easy claim ("some networks include causal and normative relations"). The strong claim: some explanations are unavailable unless causal and normative relations are represented in one coupled structure. In those cases the projectibility of the classification depends neither on causal regularity alone nor on normative status alone, but on their patterned interaction through recognition, uptake, authority, and response.

## Four carrying cases

- Promise: obligation-generation plus reliance, demand, release, breach, excuse, repair.
- Consent: permission-generation plus scope, withdrawal, misrecognition, authorization, liability.
- Official judgment or registration: recognition that creates or alters a status, not merely tracks it.
- Discrimination: invalid status-assignment that is socially effective but not morally valid.

Triangulation: promise and consent show ordinary normative transition; official judgment shows authorized status-creation; discrimination shows entrenched misrecognition and the gap between social efficacy and moral validity.

## What this paper lets the act-kinds paper say more cleanly

A general theory of status, recognition, and validity:

- some statuses are practice-internal and valid relative to a field;
- some are morally valid whether or not a practice recognizes them;
- some assigned positions are socially effective but invalid;
- some recognitions are merely evidential;
- some recognitions are constitutive, because the practice gives them authority;
- some misrecognitions are one-off mistakes; some are entrenched regimes.

That set of distinctions is useful for the act-kinds paper, but is probably too much machinery to introduce there.
