## Synthesis

### Consensus Strengths

All four reviewers find the paper's central problem well chosen: cases where status-assignment is socially effective without moral warrant expose a real gap between causal reduction and pure normative taxonomy. Pickwick, judgment, consent, and discrimination now give the paper enough concrete grip to make the project intelligible.

All four reviewers also treat the typed-edge architecture as promising. The causal / normative-transition / constitutive / recognitional distinctions are not seen as merely decorative. In particular, the Hohfeldian duty--claim-right relation and the release/consent cases help show why non-causal order can still be projectible.

The discussion of authorized recognition is a repeated strength. Epstein, Ásta, and Hindriks all single out judgment as one of the strongest parts of the paper: a court act can purport to find a status while also altering the procedural status profile within a field.

The three-way assessment vocabulary, social efficacy / field-validity / moral warrant, is seen as a serious contribution, especially for non-ideal social ontology. Jenkins in particular sees this as the paper's best tool for saying that wrongful statuses can be socially real without becoming morally legitimate.

### Consensus Weaknesses

The main shared concern is `field-validity`. Every reviewer says it is doing too much work without enough criteria. The worry is sharpest for communal discrimination: court and contract cases have recognizable procedures, offices, and records, but it is less clear what makes a discriminatory communal standing field-valid rather than merely effective, entrenched, or widely recognized.

The second shared concern is the threshold from repeated uptake to conferred standing. Ásta, Hindriks, and Jenkins all ask when repeated mistreatment becomes a degraded standing. They want criteria for who or what confers the status, what counts as communal standing to confer, and which constraints and enablements constitute the standing.

The third shared concern is recognition. Epstein asks for a cleaner taxonomy of cue, belief/classification, recognition, uptake, conferral, and response. Ásta worries that Figure 2 compresses conferral and recognition. Jenkins adds that individual recognition, community-level conferral, and official classification need to be distinguished.

The fourth concern is the relation between the network and existing social ontology. Epstein presses the grounding/anchoring question most explicitly: are normative-transition edges grounding relations, are fields anchors, or is the network a higher-level explanatory representation of already grounded facts? Hindriks gives the institutional version: what do functions, offices, and authority contribute over and above rules plus causal mechanisms?

The fifth concern is moral warrant. Jenkins argues that “morality rejects it” is too thin for oppression: the account should say more about domination, subordination, unjust constraint, blocked possibility, credibility, vulnerability, or loss of authority. Ásta similarly worries that moral warrant / field-validity / social efficacy need sharper conditions.

### Contradictions and Differences

The reviewers do not disagree about the paper's basic viability. All four give `Revise & Resubmit`, and all four treat the core architecture as publishable with focused revision.

They differ in what they think must be fixed first. Epstein prioritizes the ontology of the network relative to grounding and anchoring. Ásta prioritizes the conferral threshold and Figure 2. Hindriks prioritizes field-validity and institutional functions. Jenkins prioritizes the non-ideal social ontology of oppression: moral warrant, constraints, and enablements.

There is also a productive tension over `field-validity`. Epstein and Hindriks want stronger criteria for fields and authority. Jenkins worries that “field-valid” may grant oppressive communal practices too much standing unless resistant counter-practices and contestation are handled. Ásta worries that field-validity in communal discrimination may collapse into mere efficacy unless successful conferral is defined separately.

### Prioritized Revision List

1. Define `field` and `field-validity` earlier and more sharply. Add criteria or a compact diagnostic table distinguishing social efficacy, field-validity, successful conferral, and moral warrant across false promise, mistaken judgment, wrongful communal conferral, and suppressed entitlement.

2. Clarify the conferral threshold in discrimination. Say when repeated uptake becomes a conferred communal standing, who or what confers it, what counts as standing to confer, and which constraints and enablements constitute the status.

3. Disaggregate recognition. Distinguish cue/signal, belief/classification, recognition, uptake, authorized recognition, and conferral. The paper can keep the four edge types, but it should prevent “recognition” from carrying all these roles at once.

4. Explain the network's relation to grounding, anchoring, and institutional function. State whether the network is an ontological structure, an explanatory representation of already grounded facts, or a model that integrates grounding, recognition, and causal uptake.

5. Strengthen the moral-warrant account only as much as needed. The paper need not solve moral theory, but it should say what kind of moral defect is at issue in wrongful conferral: domination, subordination, unjust constraint, loss of authority/credibility, vulnerability, or blocked practical possibility.

6. Revise Figure 2 or its surrounding prose so the ascribed standing visibly includes constraints and enablements, not only downstream exclusion/sanction/harm. This may be prose rather than another graph.

7. Consider a compact table rather than another full diagram. A table could handle field-validity, successful conferral, efficacy, and warrant without increasing figure burden.

## Raw Reviews

### Brian Epstein

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social statuses such as promises, consent, judgments, and discriminatory standings are best explained as field-indexed causal-normative networks that combine causal uptake, normative transitions, constitutive correlatives, and recognition while separating social efficacy, field-validity, and moral warrant.

2. **Strengths**

- The core problem is well chosen: cases where a status assignment is socially effective without being morally warranted are exactly where reductive accounts strain. The Pickwick/false-promise case and the discrimination case are usefully distinguished rather than forced into one template.

- The four dependence types are philosophically promising. The duty/claim-right relation is rightly treated as constitutive rather than causal, and the paper is careful not to identify recognition, uptake, and warrant.

- The “authorized recognition” discussion is one of the strongest parts. It captures a real feature of judgments and registrations: some recognitions are not merely evidence of a status but status-altering acts within a field.

3. **Weaknesses**

- The paper has not yet shown that the typed-network apparatus adds enough beyond grounding, anchoring, and causal consequence. It says the graph “doesn’t replace the grounding story,” but then the reader needs a precise account of what the graph contributes ontologically. Are normative-transition edges grounding relations? Are fields anchors of frame principles? Or is the network just an explanatory diagram overlaying familiar relations?

- “Field” does too much work. Field-validity, authorized recognition, response licensing, and the repertoire of statuses all depend on the field, but the account gives no clear criteria for individuating fields or resolving contested fields. In cases of oppressive or resistant counter-practice, when exactly is an assignment field-valid rather than merely effective?

- Recognition remains unstable across the paper. Sometimes recognition tracks an already obtaining status; sometimes it misfires; sometimes authorized recognition creates a status; in the discrimination case, recognition seems partly constitutive of the standing. The paper needs a cleaner taxonomy of cue, belief/classification, recognition, uptake, conferral, and response.

4. **Key Q&A Question**

What exactly is the relation between your “field” and an Epstein-style anchoring story: are fields the anchors of status-generating principles, the social mechanisms that stabilize those anchors, or the normative frames whose conditions are anchored by something else?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The paper has a strong problem and a promising structure, but for *Journal of Social Ontology* it needs to say more sharply what the network ontology adds beyond a combined account of grounding, anchoring, recognition, and causal effects.

### Ásta

**Source Note**

I read `main.tex` fully. I did not read the cited sources themselves in the repo, so any comment about fit with a named source, including Ásta’s conferralism, should be treated as needing verification against the source.

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social and institutional statuses are projectible only when modeled as causal-normative networks: typed structures joining normative transitions, constitutive deontic relations, recognition, and causal uptake while distinguishing social efficacy, field-validity, and moral warrant.

2. **Strengths**

- The paper clearly avoids the most tempting collapse: it distinguishes false recognition, where no status obtains, from wrongful conferral, where a status can be socially real and still morally indefensible. That distinction is essential for handling discrimination.

- The typed-edge model is genuinely useful. Causal, constitutive, normative-transition, and recognitional relations are doing different explanatory work, and the promise/release/consent examples make that visible.

- The treatment of authorized recognition is strong. The court judgment case shows why some recognitions don’t merely track status but also alter a status profile within a field.

3. **Weaknesses**

- The discrimination case still risks sliding into recognition-only construction. The paper says entrenched uptake “helps generate a degraded standing,” but it needs a sharper account of when repeated uptake becomes a conferred communal status rather than merely repeated mistreatment. Specify who or what confers the status, what counts as communal standing to confer, and which constraints and enablements constitute the status.

- “Field-validity” is underdefined in the hardest cases. It works for courts and contracts, but in communal discrimination “field-valid” can sound like either mere social efficacy, internal practice-validity, or genuine status validity without moral warrant. The paper should add a compact diagnostic table distinguishing false promise, mistaken judgment, wrongful communal conferral, and suppressed entitlement.

- Figure 2 makes “ascription” directly generate “ascribed standing,” then has the standing recognized and taken up. From a conferralist angle, that is too compressed. The model should separate the conferring practice from later recognition of the conferred status, otherwise it becomes unclear whether recognition tracks the status or constitutes it.

4. **Key Q&A Question**

In the discrimination case, what exactly makes the degraded standing real: the community’s attitudes, the durable constraints and enablements, the field’s internal rules, or the victim’s changed practical position? And how do you prevent that answer from reducing social status to mere uptake?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The paper has a strong and publishable architecture, but the conferralist machinery in the discrimination section needs tightening before the central payoff is secure.

### Frank Hindriks

**Source Note**

I read `main.tex` fully. I did not verify the bibliography or reread the cited sources, so any source-specific points below are based on the draft’s own presentation and would need checking against the cited works.

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social and institutional statuses are projectible only when represented in typed causal-normative networks that distinguish causal uptake, normative transition, constitutive correlativity, and recognition, while separately assessing social efficacy, field-validity, and moral warrant.

2. **Strengths**

- The paper understands the central social-ontological problem well: false promise, mistaken judgment, and discrimination show why neither “status is just treatment” nor “status is just a normative fact” can explain the phenomena. The Pickwick setup is especially effective because it forces the distinction between being treated as bound and actually being bound.

- The four-edge framework is promising and institutionally plausible in outline. The distinction between causal uptake, normative transition, constitutive Hohfeldian correlativity, and recognition gives the paper a real model rather than a loose metaphor.

- The discussion of judgment as “authorized recognition” is one of the strongest parts. It shows that an official act can both purport to find a status and alter the procedural status landscape. That is exactly the kind of mixed mechanism a Journal of Social Ontology audience will want handled.

3. **Weaknesses**

- “Field-validity” is not yet sharp enough. The paper says field-validity depends on “the field’s own generating conditions,” but for contested or oppressive communal practices it is unclear what counts as a field, whose rules define it, and when repeated uptake becomes validity rather than merely entrenched treatment. This is especially risky in the discrimination section: if degraded standing is “field-valid,” the paper needs to say why this isn’t just recognition-collapse with a new label.

- Institutional functions are underdeveloped. The field section briefly invokes status functions, authorized conferral, records, offices, and procedures, but the typed network does not yet say what functions contribute over and above rules plus causal mechanisms. A judgment, contract, diagnosis, or office-holding status is not merely a node with downstream uptake; it has a point within a practice. The model should say whether institutional function is a node type, a field parameter, a teleological constraint on transitions, or part of the mechanism that stabilizes field-validity.

- Promise and consent need more institutional variation. The draft often treats promising and consent as clean deontic transitions, but real practices include capacity, authority, defect, coercion, formality, revocability, evidentiary records, and role-sensitive powers. The consent example especially risks looking too simple: withdrawal changes permission, but institutional settings also define who may withdraw, how notice works, what emergency exceptions apply, and what records do. Add one dense case analysis showing the network handling these complications.

4. **Key Q&A Question**

When a discriminatory communal practice is entrenched enough to confer a degraded standing, what exactly makes that standing “field-valid” rather than merely socially effective, and who or what has the authority to settle the field’s validity conditions?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The core idea is strong and well suited to Journal of Social Ontology, but the submission needs a sharper account of field-validity, institutional function, and the boundary between valid status-conferral and entrenched uptake before it can carry the weight of the argument.

### Katharine Jenkins

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social and institutional statuses are projectible only when modeled as causal-normative networks with typed edges for causal uptake, normative transition, constitutive correlation, and recognition, allowing a status to be socially effective or field-valid without being morally warranted.

2. **Strengths**

- The core ontology is disciplined: the paper does not reduce obligations, permissions, claims, or standings to beliefs and behavior, but also does not treat social uptake as irrelevant noise. The four-edge structure introduced around [main.tex:70](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:70) is clear and useful.

- The distinction between social efficacy, field-validity, and moral warrant is the paper’s best contribution for non-ideal social ontology. It gives the paper a way to say that wrongful statuses can be socially real without being morally legitimate, especially in the discrimination discussion at [main.tex:232](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:232).

- The paper is careful not to claim that all oppression is misrecognition. The concession at [main.tex:248](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:248) is important: material, distributive, and coercive oppression are not swallowed by the recognition model.

3. **Weaknesses**

- “Field-validity” is doing too much work. In contract or court cases, field-validity has procedural anchors. In communal discrimination, it becomes much less clear who has standing to confer the degraded status, what makes the “field” authoritative, and how resistant counter-practices affect validity. Without sharper criteria, “valid-in-field” risks collapsing into “socially entrenched enough to matter.” Add a focused clarification after [main.tex:300](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:300): what exactly makes an oppressive communal standing field-valid rather than merely effective?

- The moral-warrant distinction is promising but currently too thin. The paper says moral warrant is absent, but it does not yet say enough about what makes a conferred standing wrongful beyond “morality rejects it.” For oppression, that is not enough. The account needs a more substantive connection to domination, subordination, unjust constraint, loss of authority, credibility, vulnerability, or blocked practical possibility. Otherwise oppression looks like “unwarranted status plus harm,” not an unjust organization of social possibilities.

- Constraints and enablements are mentioned, but they are not yet structurally central. The discrimination figure moves from ascription to standing to uptake to exclusion/sanction/harm at [main.tex:254](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:254), but the “real constraints and enablements” that supposedly constitute the standing remain offstage. For this paper’s purposes, they should be represented as part of the standing’s profile, not merely downstream effects.

Source-grounding note: the empirical claims about callback rates and car negotiations at [main.tex:273](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:273) need verification against the cited sources; I have not read those sources here.

4. **Key Q&A Question**

When a discriminatory standing is communally conferred and morally wrongful, in virtue of what is it field-valid rather than merely socially effective, and how do resistant counter-practices alter or contest that validity?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The paper has a strong and original architecture, but for Journal of Social Ontology it needs a sharper account of field-validity, moral warrant, and the constitutive role of constraints and enablements in oppression.
