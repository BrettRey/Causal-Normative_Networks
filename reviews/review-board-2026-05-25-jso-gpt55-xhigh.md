## Executive Synthesis

All four reviewers recommend **Revise and Resubmit**. They agree that the paper has a publishable core for JSO: the forced-choice setup is strong, the typed-edge apparatus is promising, and the distinction among social efficacy, field-validity, and moral warrant is the central contribution. The main obstacle is not that the project is weak, but that the key social-ontology machinery is still under-specified.

## Consensus Strengths

- The opening problem is well chosen: a status-assignment can be socially powerful without thereby being warranted.
- The causal / constitutive / normative-transition / recognitional typology is seen as useful and not merely decorative.
- The paper’s anti-reductionist target is compelling: neither causal regularity alone nor deontic taxonomy alone explains the cases.
- The discrimination and misrecognition material is the right payoff zone, especially the refusal to infer legitimacy from successful social conferral.
- The counterfactual-signature strategy helps keep the network from looking like a loose diagram.

## Consensus Weaknesses

- **Field-validity is underdefined.** Reviewers repeatedly ask what a field is, what fixes its rules, who has authority within it, and how field-validity differs from entrenched uptake.
- **Recognition is overloaded.** It currently covers tracking, uptake, official recording, false recognition, communal conferral, and authorized status-making.
- **Projectibility is overextended.** Causal prediction, constitutive read-off, normative implication, and recognitional expectation are different kinds of inference.
- **The Epstein challenge is too late and too weak.** The paper must say whether causal-normative networks represent grounded social facts, help ground them, or integrate grounding/anchoring with uptake.
- **Wrongful conferral needs conditions.** The paper must explain when repeated biased recognition becomes a genuinely conferred degraded standing rather than widespread false recognition.
- **Moral warrant is too thin.** “Morally void,” “baseless,” “without warrant,” “unjust,” and “without moral authority” are not interchangeable.
- **Discrimination/oppression claims outrun the argument.** Wrongful conferral should be framed as a mechanism of some discrimination/oppression, not as discrimination or oppression as such.

## Productive Tensions

- Epstein wants the paper to foreground grounding/anchoring and decide whether the network is ontology or representation. Hindriks wants institutional functions and mechanisms made explicit. These are compatible: an early “fields and anchoring” paragraph could support both.
- Ásta and Jenkins both like wrongful conferral but warn that “morally void” risks denying the reality and moral significance of oppressive statuses. The phrase should probably become “morally unwarranted,” “without moral authority,” or “unjustified” except in false-promise cases.
- Hindriks wants the four carrying cases worked through in one schema. Jenkins and Ásta warn that promise cannot model race/gender conferral without level distinctions. A compact matrix may solve this if it distinguishes false recognition, institutional validity, communal conferral, and suppressed entitlement.

## Prioritized Revision List

1. **Define field and field-validity in Section 1.** Give contrast cases: effective but not field-valid; field-valid but morally unwarranted; morally warranted but unrecognized.
2. **Disaggregate recognition.** Separate epistemic tracking, social uptake, official recording, communal conferral, and authorized status-making recognition.
3. **State how the network relates to grounding and anchoring.** Make Epstein-style grounding/anchoring a central confrontation, not a late aside.
4. **Rebuild Section 6 around a matrix.** Distinguish false recognition, wrongful communal conferral, unjust institutional validity, and suppressed entitlement.
5. **Specify conditions for communal conferral.** Include relevant community, standing, context, base-property ascription, threshold, and constraints/enablings.
6. **Replace overbroad moral language.** Use “morally unwarranted” or “without moral authority” for real but unjust statuses; reserve “void” for status-nullity cases.
7. **Clarify projectibility.** Distinguish causal prediction, constitutive read-off, normative implication, and recognitional expectation.
8. **Use Ásta’s constraints/enablings machinery fully.** Show how wrongful conferral restructures agency, entitlement, risk, credibility, opportunity, and advantage.
9. **Keep audit studies in their lane.** Let them establish signal-recognition-response pathways, then add a separate argument for conferred standing.
10. **Add targeted non-ideal social ontology only if needed.** Ásta is necessary but likely not enough for JSO; any additional sources need verification before citation.

# Full Reviewer Outputs

## Brian Epstein / Herschel

Reviewed read-only.

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social statuses such as promises, judgments, consent, and discriminatory standings require a typed causal-normative network, because neither ordinary causal graphs nor bare normative taxonomies can explain how a status-assignment can be socially effective, field-valid, and yet morally unwarranted.

2. **Strengths**

- The central diagnostic case is well chosen: misrecognition lets the paper show why efficacy, field-validity, and moral warrant must be separated rather than treated as one status property.
- The four-edge typology is clear enough to be usable: causal, constitutive, normative-transition, and recognitional dependence do different explanatory jobs.
- The paper is at its best in the discrimination discussion, especially where it says audit studies measure patterned uptake rather than the metaphysics or wrongness of race discrimination.

3. **Weaknesses**

- The paper hasn’t yet shown that the typed-network apparatus adds more than a redescription of grounding, anchoring, constitutive rules, and causal uptake. The Epstein-style challenge is acknowledged only late, around [main.tex:257](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:257), but not answered.
- “Recognition” is doing too many jobs: tracking, uptake, official recording, misrecognition, and status-making authorized recognition. These need to be separated more sharply, or the misrecognition case becomes unstable.
- “Projectibility” is overextended. Inductive projection, normative consequence, constitutive correlation, and causal prediction are not the same kind of inference, but the paper sometimes treats them as if they all “run along edges.”

4. **Key Question**

What exactly is the ontological relation represented by a normative-transition edge: grounding, anchoring, constitutive-rule satisfaction, practical-normative consequence, or something else?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit**: the paper has a promising and JSO-relevant thesis, but the current draft must clarify whether it is offering a new ontology of social facts or a useful diagrammatic overlay on existing social-ontological machinery.

6. **Detailed Comments For The Author**

The paper’s best version is this: social facts are not exhausted either by causal regularities of recognition and response or by deontic facts stated independently of practice. A promise, judgment, consent, or discriminatory standing has a mixed profile: some relations make or alter the status, some correlate positions within the status, some track or mis-track it, and some transmit its social effects. That is a serious point, and it is worth publishing if the ontology is made explicit.

The main problem is that “network” currently functions more as an explanatory diagram than as an ontology. At [main.tex:122](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:122), the paper says the vertices are property instances and states, and the edges are typed relations of dependence. But for social ontology, the crucial question is not only what depends on what. It is what makes the relevant social fact obtain. For example: what grounds the fact that an undertaking creates a duty? What anchors the frame principle that this sort of undertaking has that status-generating role? What makes one recognizer authorized and another merely causally influential?

This is where the Epstein comparison needs to move from a late aside to a central confrontation. The “field” section says that frame principles are anchored and don’t reduce to current uptake, which is exactly the right issue. But if fields are doing the anchoring work, then the network seems downstream of anchoring rather than a replacement for it. The paper should state whether causal-normative networks are: first, a representation of already-grounded social facts; second, part of the grounds of those facts; or third, a higher-level explanatory structure that integrates grounding, anchoring, recognition, and causal propagation.

The recognition edge is the most urgent technical problem. At [main.tex:136](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:136), recognition “tracks” a status, but can fit, mis-fit, or have no status to track. If there is no status, then the edge cannot literally run from status to recognition. False recognition needs a different structure: perhaps a representation-as relation from recognition to purported status, plus causal edges from signals or records to recognition, plus an assessment of fit. Otherwise the false-promise case is modeled with an edge from an absent relatum.

Similarly, authorized recognition is underdescribed. The claim that a judgment is “recognition plus transition” is plausible, but it needs criteria. Is the recognitional component epistemic, procedural, declarative, or merely classificatory? Is the transition produced by the judge’s authority, by the procedural rule, by institutional acceptance, or by the legal system’s anchoring conditions? Without that, “compound edge” risks hiding the very ontology the paper needs to expose.

The treatment of projectibility also needs tightening. The paper says at [main.tex:107](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:107) that projectibility is a fact about practice, not entailment. But later it allows projection from duty to claim-right across a constitutive tie and from breach to liability or repair across normative-transition relations. These are different inferential relations. A claim-right follows from a duty in one sense; enforcement may be predicted from a judgment in another; callback discrimination may be statistically projected from racialized signals in yet another. The paper should distinguish inferential, metaphysical, normative, and statistical projection.

The modularity argument in Section 5 is promising but too quick. Changing a status-rule while holding recognition and uptake fixed may be possible in an artificial contrast case, but in many social practices the rule, its recognition, and its uptake are co-anchored. The paper should say whether these “signatures” are metaphysical counterfactuals, interventionist models, contrastive explanations, or heuristic probes.

The moral-warrant layer is important, but it also threatens to pull the paper away from social ontology into moral theory. “Morally void” and “morally warranted” need a lighter, more precise treatment. The social ontological claim should be: a status can be socially real, institutionally valid, and morally defective. The paper does not need to settle the moral basis of warrant, but it does need to say whether moral warrant is itself a status in some field, an external evaluative property, or a distinct normative fact.

Citation note: I only relied on sources already cited in the manuscript and checked locally in `references-standalone.bib`. The numerical discrimination claims from Bertrand and Mullainathan should still be rechecked against the article before submission, since they carry argumentative weight.

7. **What Would Make This Publishable In JSO**

1. Put the grounding/anchoring challenge up front: explain how causal-normative networks relate to Epstein-style grounding and anchoring, not just Khalidi-style causal graphs.

2. Disaggregate recognition into at least three relations: epistemic tracking, social uptake, and authorized status-making recognition.

3. Clarify the ontology of normative-transition edges: say whether they are grounding relations, rule-mediated status changes, powers, or sui generis normative transitions.

4. Replace the broad “projection along edges” claim with a typology of inferences licensed by different edge types.

5. Make fields do real work earlier: define what fixes field-validity, who or what anchors authorization, and how field-validity differs from moral warrant.

6. Add one fully worked case besides promising, preferably official judgment or discrimination, showing every node and edge and where validity, efficacy, and warrant diverge.

7. Reduce the claim about causal graphs: the paper should not say social ontology needs causal graphs generally; it should say some explanations of socially effective but unwarranted status require a representation that keeps causal uptake and normative status in one typed structure.

## Ásta / Euler

Source note: I checked the manuscript’s cited works locally in `references-standalone.bib`. Any additional literature I mention as desirable should be verified before citation.

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that socially effective but morally unwarranted statuses are best explained by causal-normative networks: typed structures combining causal, constitutive, normative-transition, and recognitional dependencies without reducing social status either to uptake or to freestanding normativity.

2. **Strengths**

- The core problem is well chosen for JSO: the paper sees that wrongful status-assignment has to be socially real without thereby becoming morally warranted.
- The typed-edge apparatus is genuinely useful. The distinction between causal uptake, normative transition, constitutive correlation, and recognition is not decorative; it does explanatory work across promising, judgment, and discrimination.
- The paper’s best move is in Section 6: it refuses the bad inference from “conferred” to “morally legitimate.” That is exactly the right direction for handling oppressive or discriminatory status.

3. **Weaknesses**

- The account of wrongful communal conferral is underdefined. The paper needs to say who has standing to confer a communal status, what threshold of uptake counts, and how successful wrongful conferral differs from widespread false recognition.
- The efficacy / field-validity / moral-warrant distinction is promising but unstable. “Morally void,” “baseless,” “valid-in-field,” and “real within its community” are doing different jobs and sometimes pull against one another.
- The discrimination case moves too quickly from audit-study evidence of differential treatment to the metaphysical claim that discrimination is entrenched wrongful conferral. The evidence supports recognitional uptake; it does not by itself establish conferred standing.

4. **Key Question**

In the discrimination case, what distinguishes a genuinely conferred degraded communal status from a merely widespread pattern of mistaken recognition, biased treatment, and sanction?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The paper has a publishable core, but the central conferralist machinery needs sharper conditions before JSO readers will trust the discrimination and wrongful-status payoff.

6. **Detailed Comments For The Author**

The opening is strong. The wrongly treated promisor/debtor case makes the target vivid, and the forced-choice framing is charitable: neither causal reduction nor pure normative taxonomy can explain both social efficacy and lack of warrant. But the promise example is also misleading if it remains the dominant model for the whole paper. A false promise-ascription is not yet the same structure as wrongful communal conferral. In the promise case, the relevant status fails to obtain. In the discrimination case, you later want the degraded standing to be genuinely conferred. That contrast should be made explicit earlier.

The abstract and Section 1 introduce “field-validity” before the reader knows what a field is. Since the paper’s whole payoff depends on field-validity coming apart from moral warrant, the notion needs at least a preliminary definition in the opening. Otherwise “valid within a field” risks sounding like “accepted by the relevant people,” which is exactly the recognition-only view you reject.

The four edge types are a real contribution. The discussion of Hohfeldian correlatives as constitutive rather than causal is clean, and the Woodwardian intervention contrast helps. Still, the recognitional edge is too often described as tracking a status. That works for ordinary recognition and misrecognition, but it is not enough for conferralism. In communal conferral, recognition or ascription can be part of what brings the status into being. The model needs a distinct account of conferral as a status-generating transition, not merely recognition followed by causal uptake.

This matters most around the sentence: “Conferral constitutes a social status, real within its community and fully effective, but it confers no moral warrant.” That is close to right, but too compressed. Conferral is not just “enough recognition.” It depends on context, standing, base property ascription, and the constraints and enablements imposed. If the paper does not spell this out, the account will look like recognition-only construction with a moral assessment added afterward.

The moral-warrant language also needs tightening. “Morally void” suggests nullity, as if the status is not real after all. But your own view needs the status to be real and morally defective. Prefer “morally unwarranted,” “without moral authority,” or “unjustified” unless you specifically mean invalidity. “Baseless” is also dangerous: a wrongful status can be wrong even when the perceived base property is correctly ascribed. The wrong may lie in the social constraints attached to it, the community’s use of that property, or the field’s unjust rules.

The three-way distinction in Section 6 should probably become a more explicit matrix. False recognition: uptake without successful status-conferral. Wrongful communal conferral: successful conferral without moral warrant. Unjust institutional validity: field-valid status under bad rules. Suppressed entitlement: moral warrant without recognition or field uptake. Right now these are named, but the conditions are not stable enough.

The discrimination discussion is good in its caution about audit studies. You correctly say the studies measure patterned uptake, not the metaphysics of race or discrimination. But then the paper immediately concludes that discrimination is entrenched wrongful conferral. That conclusion needs an intermediate argument: how do repeated recognition-response pathways impose constraints and enablements sufficient for a degraded standing? Otherwise the audit studies support only the causal-recognitional part of your network.

The “field” section should come earlier or be partially previewed earlier. The institutional/communal distinction is exactly what the paper needs, but it arrives after the main conferralist claims have already been made. The paper should define institutional field-validity and communal successful-conferral separately. A court judgment is not valid in the same way a communal gender or race status is successfully conferred.

Be careful with the claim that discrimination is communal because there is “no authorizing office.” In conferralism, communal statuses can still involve standing. Lack of office does not mean lack of authorization in every sense. The relevant question is: what gives a community standing to confer, and can that standing itself be morally defective? This is where your efficacy / field-validity / moral-warrant distinction could become powerful, but it needs more explicit machinery.

Figure 2 is helpful but slightly misleading. It draws moral warrant as an “Assess” node while the caption says moral warrant is not a status in the network. If moral warrant is external assessment, represent it as such. Also, “effective, valid-in-field” on the figure is too strong unless you have already established what field-validity means for wrongful communal conferral.

Finally, the conclusion says oppressive law renders standing effective and field-valid, but the discrimination section focuses mainly on hiring audit studies and communal conferral. Either bring legal discrimination into the body as an institutional case, or make the conclusion match the communal case more closely.

7. **What Would Make This Publishable In JSO**

1. Define the assessment vocabulary early: social efficacy, successful conferral, field-validity, and moral warrant. Do not make field-validity carry both institutional validity and communal conferral success unless you explicitly defend that.

2. Rebuild Section 6 around the contrast between false recognition and wrongful conferral. This is the paper’s decisive issue.

3. Specify communal conferral conditions: relevant community, standing, base-property ascription, context, threshold, and imposed constraints/enablers.

4. Revise the discrimination case so audit studies establish only the signal-recognition-response pathway; then add a separate argument for when such pathways amount to degraded conferred standing.

5. Replace “morally void” and “baseless” where they suggest nonexistence. The target claim should be: real status, real constraints, no moral authority.

6. Add verified engagement with the feminist social ontology literature on social positions, oppression, race, and gender construction. The Ásta citation is necessary but not enough for JSO.

## Frank Hindriks / Rawls

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social statuses such as promises, consent, judgments, and discriminatory standings are best explained as nodes in typed causal-normative networks whose causal, constitutive, recognitional, and normative-transition relations jointly explain how a status can be socially effective, field-valid, and yet morally unwarranted.

2. **Strengths**

- The core forced-choice diagnosis is strong: the paper clearly shows why neither causal regularity alone nor deontic taxonomy alone can explain cases where status and uptake come apart.
- The efficacy / field-validity / moral warrant distinction is promising and well suited to Journal of Social Ontology debates, especially in the misrecognition and discrimination sections.
- The counterfactual “signature” strategy is a good way to stop the typed network from looking like a loose diagram; it gives the edge-types different explanatory behavior.

3. **Weaknesses**

- **Field-validity is doing too much work without sharp truth-conditions.** The paper says an assignment is field-valid when it satisfies “the rules of a practice,” but it doesn’t yet say what makes something a field, what counts as its rules, or how validity differs from entrenched uptake.
- **Institutions, functions, and authority enter too late.** Searle, Epstein, Ásta, and authorized recognition appear mainly in §§6–7, but the institutional machinery is needed earlier to explain why judgments, registrations, contracts, and medical consent have the powers the network attributes to them.
- **Projectibility risks equivocation.** Causal prediction, constitutive read-off, normative implication, and recognitional expectation are all treated as “projection,” but they are not the same epistemic relation. The paper needs to distinguish them or defend the generalized usage.

4. **Key Question**

What exactly makes a status-assignment field-valid rather than merely socially effective, and how do institutional functions, authority-conferring rules, and social mechanisms fix that validity within the network?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit**: the paper has a publishable core, but JSO readers will need a sharper account of field-validity, institutional functions, and the mechanisms that make the typed network explanatory rather than diagrammatic.

6. **Detailed Comments for the Author**

I take the paper’s central achievement to be this: it identifies a genuine explanatory gap between causal accounts of social regularity and purely normative accounts of status. A person wrongly treated as bound is not simply bound; but the treatment is also not epiphenomenal. That is the right problem. The proposed typed network is a plausible answer because it lets the author represent status-generation, recognition, uptake, and response without reducing one relation-type to another.

The opening section works well as a problem statement, but it introduces “field-validity” before the notion of a field is available. The reader is told that some statuses are “valid within a field without being just,” but not yet what kind of object a field is. Is a field a practice, an institution, a community, a legal order, a professional domain, or any norm-governed setting? The later answer in §7 is helpful, but too late and too loose. This concept is central enough to require an early stipulative definition.

The promise case is clean, but it is not obviously institutional in the same sense as judgment or registration. Promising may be a moral-normative practice, a conventional speech-act practice, or a legal-contractual practice, depending on the case. Consent likewise differs across sexual ethics, medicine, contract, and criminal law. The paper currently moves between these domains too easily. The author should say, case by case, which field is in play and what its validity conditions are.

The discussion of Khalidi is effective. The extension from causal order to typed order is clear, and the paper is right that moral and institutional statuses require more than ordinary causal edges. I checked the Khalidi citation locally in `references-standalone.bib`. Still, the paper should be more careful about the term “projectibility.” From “this is gold” one projects empirical properties. From “this is a duty” one reads off a correlative claim-right. From “this is a valid judgment” one may predict enforcement. These are different inferential moves. Calling all of them projectibility may be defensible, but the defense must be explicit.

The critique of normative taxonomies is also plausible, especially against the idea that Hohfeldian structure alone explains social cascades. The manuscript’s Hohfeld citation is locally present. But the section risks setting up a target no Hohfeldian need accept: of course a taxonomy alone does not explain recognition, enforcement, reliance, or sanction. The stronger point is not that taxonomy is incomplete, but that institutional explanation needs both deontic structure and mechanisms of uptake. That mechanism-sensitive claim should be foregrounded.

The typed-edge model is the heart of the paper. The four edge-types are useful, but the node inventory is too heterogeneous. “Field structures” are listed as nodes in §4, but §7 treats fields as parameters fixing which nodes and edges count. These cannot both be the default picture without further explanation. If fields are parameters, say so. If they are nodes, show how a field-node connects to authority, recognition, and transition edges.

Authorized recognition needs more analysis. Saying that a court judgment is recognition plus normative transition is close, but it hides the institutional function that makes the recognition authoritative. A judgment does not alter status merely because someone recognizes something; it does so because an office-holder, under a procedure, within an institution, performs an act with a status function. The network needs a place for that authority-conferring structure.

The counterfactual signatures in §5 are one of the paper’s best devices. But “release the promisor and the same non-performance stops being a breach, so demand, blame, and repair lapse” should be qualified. Normatively they lapse; causally they may continue if the release is not recognized. That is precisely the paper’s own point. The section should distinguish licensed response from actual response.

The judgment example is institutionally under-specified. Some judgments declare pre-existing rights; some create new procedural positions; some make enforcement available; some are mistaken but still legally operative. The paper’s claim that “a judgment creates the liability” is too broad unless “liability” is meant as a new procedural/remedial position. The Restatement citation is locally present, but the legal claim still needs tightening.

The misrecognition section is the strongest section, but it also exposes the biggest unresolved issue. “Wrongful field-validity” is a powerful category, but what is the field in a discriminatory case? Law? Workplace practice? Racialized communal status? A racist institutional order? If a discriminatory assignment is “field-valid,” the author must prevent readers from hearing “valid” as normatively sanitized. “Internally valid relative to field F” may be better than “field-valid” unless the term is defined with great care.

The use of Ásta is apt and locally grounded in the bibliography. The paper correctly sees that wrongful conferral can be real without being morally warranted. But it should say more about how communal conferral differs from institutional conferral. In the institutional case, offices, procedures, and status functions matter. In the communal case, authority is diffuse and may not be office-based. That distinction should not be confined to §7.

The discrimination discussion is careful in noting that audit studies measure patterned uptake rather than the metaphysics of race or discrimination. The cited Bertrand and Mullainathan, Ayres, Weinberger, Kohler-Hausmann, and Hu entries are all locally present. The section’s philosophical point is good: signal manipulation can reveal recognitional-causal pathways without defining the social kind. But the paper should connect this more directly to field-validity. Are racialized ascriptions field-valid in oppressive communal fields, or merely effective? That distinction matters.

The fields section should be moved earlier or split. The current §7 arrives after the main machinery has already relied on fields for validity, authority, recognition, and response. JSO readers will want the institutional ontology before the payoff. The section also needs more on functions. Searle is cited locally, but “status functions by authorized conferral” is too compressed. What function does the status perform in the practice? How does that function stabilize expectations and mechanisms? How does it differ from mere classification?

The conclusion is good but slightly overstates what has been shown. The paper has shown that some explanations benefit from representing causal and normative relations together. To show that they are “unavailable” otherwise, the author needs the sharper field-validity and function story above. Once that is supplied, the stronger conclusion will be credible.

7. **What Would Make This Publishable In JSO**

1. Define “field” and “field-validity” early, with explicit truth-conditions and contrast cases: effective but not field-valid; field-valid but morally unwarranted; morally warranted but institutionally unrecognized.

2. Integrate institutional functions into the typed model, not only the final section. Add a compact account of office, authority, procedure, status function, and authorized recognition.

3. Distinguish kinds of projection: causal prediction, normative implication, constitutive correlation, and recognitional expectation. Either reserve “projectibility” for the mixed package or defend the generalized use.

4. Work the four carrying cases through the same schema: promise, consent, judgment/registration, discrimination. A short table would help.

5. Tighten the legal and institutional examples, especially judgment, consent, release, and liability.

6. Make the mechanisms explicit: records, monitoring, enforcement routines, reputational sanctions, professional protocols, and habits of uptake.

7. Remove remaining TODO material and settle whether the title foregrounds “field-validity,” “status,” or “causal-normative networks.”

## Katharine Jenkins / Bernoulli

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social and moral statuses are best explained as nodes in typed causal-normative networks, where causal uptake, normative transitions, recognitional tracking, and constitutive ties explain how a status can be socially effective or field-valid while lacking moral warrant.

2. **Strengths**

- The core forced-choice framing is strong: the paper convincingly shows that neither causal regularity alone nor deontic taxonomy alone captures cases where status, uptake, and warrant come apart.
- The distinction between social efficacy, field-validity, and moral warrant is promising and potentially valuable for non-ideal social ontology.
- The recognition-signature idea gives the paper a clear diagnostic tool: changing recognition while holding status fixed nicely exposes what reductive causal and reductive normative accounts miss.

3. **Weaknesses**

- The oppression and discrimination claims currently outrun the argument. “Wrongful conferral” is a useful mechanism, but the manuscript sometimes treats it as if it captures discrimination or oppression as such.
- “Moral warrant” is too thin. The paper needs to say what makes an assignment morally warranted or unwarranted: legitimacy, justice, authority, respect, non-domination, entitlement, or something else.
- Ásta’s constraints-and-enablement machinery is invoked but not really used. The paper needs to show how wrongful conferral structures practical possibilities, not merely how it produces recognitional uptake and downstream harm.

4. **Key Question**

If a socially conferred race or gender status is real because it structures constraints and enablements, what exactly makes that status “morally void” rather than morally real but unjust, oppressive, contested, or resistantly reappropriated?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit**: the paper has a publishable core, but for JSO the non-ideal social ontology has to be much more developed, especially around oppression, wrongful conferral, and moral warrant.

6. **Detailed Comments For The Author**

The paper’s central contribution is clear and worth pursuing. I understand the argument as follows. You begin with cases where a person is treated as having a status they lack, or as bound by a demand that has social force but no warrant. You then argue that such cases require a structure with several dependence types: causal dependence, constitutive dependence, normative-transition dependence, and recognitional dependence. Khalidi’s causal-network model supplies the projectibility demand, but moral and institutional kinds require non-causal forms of order. Hohfeldian or deontic taxonomy captures the normative structure, but not the recognitional and causal cascade by which statuses become socially efficacious. The resulting model is a typed causal-normative network, and misrecognition/discrimination are the test cases because they show efficacy without warrant.

That is a good paper. But the current draft is still much stronger on the network architecture than on discrimination and oppression.

The main problem is the move from misrecognition to oppression. In Section 6, you write that “some oppressive regimes work through stabilized wrongful conferral” and then that “discrimination is that regime case.” The first claim is careful and plausible. The second is too strong. Discrimination can operate through wrongful conferral, but also through resource allocation, exclusionary design, statistical sorting, inherited institutional routines, proxy criteria, spatial segregation, bureaucratic indifference, and material coercion. Some of these involve recognition; some do not centrally involve conferral of a degraded standing. The paper should either restrict its claim to a subset of discrimination or argue that these other cases still depend on status-conferral in the relevant sense.

The distinction between false recognition, wrongful field-validity, and suppressed entitlement is one of the most promising parts of the paper. It also needs more work. False recognition is well illustrated by the false promise case. Wrongful field-validity is gestured at through discrimination. Suppressed entitlement is introduced but not developed. Yet suppressed entitlement may be crucial for gender, race, disability, and citizenship cases: the person may have the moral basis for recognition, standing, credibility, authority, or protection, while the field refuses to register it. That category could connect the paper to ontic injustice and non-ideal social ontology much more directly.

“Moral warrant” is doing too much work without enough theory. At present, moral warrant means roughly “the assignment has the right moral basis.” But what is the relevant moral basis? In promising, perhaps consent or undertaking. In contract, perhaps legitimate authority plus procedural validity. In race and gender, the issue is different: many socially real statuses are morally problematic not because they are simply baseless, but because they are structured by domination, hierarchy, exploitation, exclusion, or misclassification. “Morally void” may be apt for a false debt, but it is too blunt for oppressive social statuses. Oppressive statuses are not moral nullities. They generate moral claims, duties of resistance or repair, responsibilities of institutions, and altered practical reasons for those positioned by them.

This matters especially for the paper’s treatment of Ásta. The manuscript cites Ásta 2018 locally in `references-standalone.bib`; I am treating that as checked locally, not externally verified. You use Ásta to say that conferred social statuses consist in real constraints and enablements. But the paper then moves quickly back to recognition, uptake, exclusion, sanction, and harm. The constraints-and-enablement apparatus should do more. What constraints are imposed by the wrongful standing? What enablements accrue to others? What actions become harder, riskier, presumptively inappropriate, unavailable, or unintelligible? Oppression is not just harmful treatment following recognition; it is a patterned restructuring of agency and social possibility.

The recognition-signature claim also needs refinement in race and gender cases. In the promise case, it is plausible to hold the status fixed while recognition varies: someone either promised or did not, and others may misrecognize that. But on a conferralist account of race and gender statuses, recognition is not merely tracking an independently fixed status. Some recognition practices help constitute the status. The paper can handle this, but it needs to distinguish levels: one employer’s belief may vary while the broader conferred social status remains fixed; community-level conferral may constitute the status; official classification may alter field-validity; individual misrecognition may produce local harms. Without that level distinction, the paper risks saying both that recognition constitutes social status and that recognition merely tracks status.

The discrimination discussion is promising but compressed. The audit-study discussion is appropriately cautious: as reported in the manuscript, Bertrand and Mullainathan’s callback figures and Ayres’s car negotiation study are used only to show patterned uptake; the local bibliography contains entries for both. The point that signal manipulation tests recognition-response pathways rather than race itself is useful. But the manuscript should not let the methodological point replace the ontology. If race is a conferred social status, then a racialized signal does not merely “point to” the category; it operates within practices that make racial positioning socially salient and materially consequential. The signal is part of the network, not just an external indicator.

The “fields” section is currently too brief for the amount of work it has to do. Field-validity is central to the paper, but “field” is introduced late and loosely. That may be fine as a stipulation, but JSO readers will ask: who fixes a field’s rules? How are fields bounded? What happens when fields conflict? Can an oppressive institution generate field-valid statuses while oppressed communities contest or reject them? Are resistant counter-practices separate fields, rival recognitional authorities, or failures of uptake? These questions matter because non-ideal social ontology is rarely tidy. Oppressive social reality is plural, contested, and unevenly enforced.

The paper should also be more careful with “valid.” “Field-valid but morally unwarranted” is a useful phrase, but “valid” has legitimating overtones. You may want a term like “internally valid,” “practice-valid,” or “rule-valid,” and then explicitly deny that this implies legitimacy. Otherwise the reader may worry that oppressive law or discriminatory practice is being granted too much normative standing.

Finally, the paper should engage more directly with social ontology of oppression. The manuscript currently cites Ásta and Fraser/Honneth locally, and those citations are present in the local bibliography. But for a JSO submission, the paper likely needs more contact with feminist and non-ideal social ontology: social construction, oppressive kinds, gender/race as social positions, ontic injustice, and structural oppression. Specific additions would need verification before use, but likely candidates include work by Haslanger, Jenkins, Mallon, Cudd, Frye, Young, Dotson, Fricker, Mason, and/or Barnes, depending on the final argumentative route.

7. **What Would Make This Publishable In JSO**

1. Clarify the scope: say explicitly whether wrongful conferral is a mechanism of some oppression/discrimination, or an account of discrimination as such. I recommend the narrower claim.

2. Define moral warrant. Distinguish moral correctness, legitimacy, justice, authority, and moral significance. Avoid treating oppressive statuses as simply “morally void” without qualification.

3. Make constraints and enablements central. Add one worked discrimination case showing how wrongful conferral restructures agency, opportunity, credibility, risk, entitlement, and advantage.

4. Refine the recognition/status relation. Distinguish individual recognition, community-level conferral, official classification, field-validity, and moral warrant.

5. Expand the fields section. Address contested fields, unjust institutions, resistant counter-practices, and why “field-validity” does not imply legitimacy.

6. Add targeted engagement with non-ideal social ontology and oppression literature, with all bibliographic details verified before inclusion.
