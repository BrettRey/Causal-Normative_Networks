## Executive Synthesis

All five reviewers recommend **Revise and Resubmit**. The board sees a publishable core: the forced-choice setup is strong, the typed-edge apparatus is doing real philosophical work, and the recent simplification of `field` mostly helped. The new bottleneck is that the simplification exposes a more exacting task: institutional field-validity, communal successful conferral, recognition, uptake, and moral warrant must be separated more sharply.

The strongest consensus is that Figure 2 and the wrongful-conferral prose still blur recognition and conferral. In communal cases, uptake may be part of what confers the standing, not merely a downstream recognition of an already conferred status. Several reviewers also say the paper must now state its relation to Epstein-style grounding and anchoring: if the graph is not a new layer of metaphysics, the paper should say whether it is an explanatory model, representational framework, or supplement to grounding/anchoring.

The Projectibility Reviewer gives the field revision the most favorable reading: `field` is no longer a Boydian HPC, and Boyd is now in the right narrow role. But even that review says the introduction and conclusion still need to cash out what the network lets us infer, predict, or generalize.

## Consensus Strengths

- The paper now clearly distinguishes false promise from discriminatory standing: one is effective misrecognition without the relevant status; the other may be a real conferred standing without moral warrant.
- The four dependence types are useful rather than decorative: causal, constitutive, normative-transition, and recognitional relations behave differently.
- The anti-reductionist setup works: causal graphs lose normative fit, while normative taxonomies lose uptake and social cascade.
- The recent field move is directionally right: fields are ordinary practices/domains that parameterize projection, not extra graph nodes or Boydian HPCs.
- The discrimination section is careful about audit studies: the studies establish patterned uptake, not race itself as a manipulable cause or a full account of conferred standing.

## Consensus Weaknesses

- **Field-validity and successful conferral are still too entangled.** Institutional validity and communal conferral have different success conditions and should not be carried by one disjunctive phrase everywhere.
- **Recognition remains unstable.** The draft uses recognition for tracking, uptake, official recording, false recognition, authorized status-making, and diffuse communal conferral.
- **Figure 2 misleads.** It suggests a single ascription generates standing and then uptake tracks it, but oppressive communal conferral often involves repeated uptake, mutual expectations, sanctions, and records in the status-generating process.
- **The relation to grounding/anchoring is under-specified.** The draft concedes that the graph does not replace grounding, but it does not yet say what the graph adds beyond frame principles plus anchors.
- **Moral warrant is too thin.** The paper says "right moral basis," but reviewers want to know whether warrant assesses the attribution, conferral act, standing, treatment, or some combination.
- **The transition from repeated biased uptake to conferred standing needs criteria.** Reviewers ask who has standing to confer, what threshold matters, and what distinguishes standing from repeated disadvantage.
- **The institutional machinery arrives too late.** The institutional/communal field distinction should organize promise, consent, judgment, and discrimination earlier.
- **Projectibility is present but under-cashed.** The paper needs a clearer ledger of what each edge type and each field lets us project.

## Productive Tensions

- Epstein wants the network's relation to grounding and anchoring made explicit. Hindriks wants fields, rules, offices, functions, records, and mechanisms moved earlier. These can be handled together by defining fields earlier as the practice architecture that fixes status conditions while the network represents the mixed explanatory profile.
- The Projectibility Reviewer says the field simplification basically worked; the other reviewers say the simplified field is now too modest for the work it carries. The reconciliation is to keep the simple reader-facing definition but add explicit roles: field as domain, validity-setter, authority-setter, projection-index, and stabilizing background.
- Ásta and Jenkins both press wrongful conferral, but from different angles. Ásta wants standing-to-confer and success conditions; Jenkins wants constraints/enablings, resistant counter-practices, suppressed entitlement, and material structures.
- Hindriks wants more institutional mechanism, especially judgment. Jenkins wants the oppression claims narrowed or expanded with non-ideal machinery. The revision should probably do one compact institutional mechanism and one compact communal mechanism, rather than expanding every case.

## Prioritized Revision List

1. **State the paper's contribution relative to grounding and anchoring.** Add an early paragraph: the network is not a replacement for grounding; it is an explanatory representation that integrates grounding conditions, recognitional fit/misfit, causal uptake, and field-relative projections.
2. **Separate recognition, uptake, and conferral.** Use distinct nodes/relations for recognition/classification, uptake/response, and status-conferring transition. Redraw or recaption Figure 2 accordingly.
3. **Define the assessment vocabulary early.** Institutional `field-validity`, communal `successful conferral`, social efficacy, moral warrant, enforceability, authority, and suppressed entitlement should be separate dimensions.
4. **Move the institutional/communal field distinction forward.** Use it before Section 6, not only after the damage is done.
5. **Give conditions for successful communal conferral.** Relevant community, standing in context, base-property ascription, threshold of uptake, durable constraints/enablings, and difference from repeated mistreatment.
6. **Make moral warrant minimal but explicit.** State that the paper does not offer a full moral theory, but in the target cases warrant concerns whether the standing or treatment has moral authority, respects equal standing, and does not impose unjust constraints.
7. **Add a projection ledger.** For each edge type and each field, say what inference it licenses: causal prediction, constitutive read-off, normative consequence, recognitional expectation, and downstream social cascade.
8. **Cash out discrimination as constraints/enablings.** The degraded standing should project altered options, credibility, risk, authority, mobility, institutional access, anticipatory self-regulation, and downstream harm.
9. **Develop one institutional mechanism.** Judgment is the best candidate: office, rule, record, authority, enforcement, uptake, finality/preclusion/remedial powers.
10. **Rewrite the conclusion around projectibility.** End with what the framework lets us infer, predict, and generalize, not only what it represents.

## Full Reviewer Outputs

## Brian Epstein

1. **One-sentence summary** of the paper's main claim

The paper argues that social statuses are best explained as nodes in typed causal-normative networks that combine causal uptake, normative transitions, recognitional relations, and constitutive ties, thereby explaining how statuses can be socially effective, field-valid or successfully conferred, and yet morally unwarranted.

2. **Strengths**

- The paper's core distinction between social efficacy, field-validity or successful conferral, and moral warrant is important and well motivated, especially in the transition from false promise to discrimination (lines 232-249).
- The critique of both reductive options is clear: ordinary causal graphs lose the normative fit relation, while purely normative taxonomies lose uptake, enforcement, reliance, and social cascade (lines 86-116).
- The promise, consent, judgment, and discrimination cases are well chosen because they force different relations into view: release, withdrawal, authorized recognition, and wrongful conferral do not have the same structure (lines 95-101, 197-211, 244-250).

3. **Weaknesses**

- The paper has not yet shown that the typed-network apparatus adds enough beyond grounding and anchoring. At line 125, the network is explicitly said not to replace the grounding story; at lines 300-302, fields are recast in Epsteinian frame/anchor terms. The paper then needs to say exactly what the network explains that a frame-principle-plus-anchor account cannot.
- "Field" is doing too much work. It names a practice, fixes validity conditions, parameterizes projection, authorizes recognition, distinguishes institutional from communal cases, and sometimes substitutes for successful conferral (lines 82-84, 294-316). These roles need to be separated.
- Recognition remains unstable. Sometimes it is a relation from status to representation (lines 139, 153); sometimes a node with causal outputs (lines 129, 203-205); sometimes authorized status creation (lines 207-211); and in Figure 2 the recognitional edge appears to run from ascribed standing to uptake rather than to a recognition state (lines 263-269). This is the distinction the paper most needs to make crisp.

4. **Key question** you would ask at Q&A

What is the precise explanatory gain of representing these cases as typed networks rather than as social facts grounded by field-relative frame principles whose anchors include records, routines, recognition, enforcement, and expectations?

5. **Verdict**: Revise & Resubmit

Promising and publishable in ambition, but not ready for JSO until the ontology of fields, recognition, and normative-transition edges is distinguished more sharply from familiar grounding and anchoring machinery.

6. **Detailed comments for the author**

The paper's best idea is that misrecognition is not one phenomenon. False promise, mistaken judgment, and discriminatory standing differ because the status may be absent, procedurally created, or communally conferred while morally defective. That taxonomy is a genuine contribution. Lines 238-249 are especially strong: they prevent the paper from sliding into either "social recognition makes it so" or "wrongful treatment is merely external noise."

The main problem is that the paper sometimes describes a new ontology and sometimes describes a diagrammatic display of already familiar relations. Line 125 says the graph is "not a new layer of metaphysics" and that frame principles fix grounding conditions while anchors explain why those principles are in place. That is the right concession, but it creates pressure. If the network is not metaphysically additional, then the paper must define its contribution as explanatory, representational, or methodological. The conclusion says some explanations are "unavailable unless the two are represented together" (lines 327), but the examples mostly show that both normative and causal facts are relevant. That is weaker than showing that typed networks are indispensable.

The normative-transition edge needs more work. At line 78, an undertaking "generates" a duty; at line 151, a performance or rule "generates, terminates, blocks, defeats, or modifies" a status. But line 125 says Nora's duty is grounded by her undertaking under the relevant field rules. Is normative-transition a grounding relation, a change-making relation, a rule-governed operation, or a formal edge in an explanatory model? If it is grounding, say so and explain why it deserves a separate graph-theoretic type. If it is not grounding, state the criterion that separates it from grounding and constitution.

The field discussion is valuable but overloaded. Lines 294-310 define a field as a practice that fixes what counts, which performances generate statuses, which records preserve them, which recognitions count, and which responses are licensed. That makes "field" close to a bundle of frame principles plus anchors. But lines 306-316 introduce communal fields where there may be no rulebook or office, and the question becomes successful conferral. That is not merely another kind of validity. I would separate "field-validity" for institutional-rule cases from "successful conferral" for communal-status cases throughout the paper, rather than repeatedly using the disjunction.

The recognition apparatus should be redrawn. In Figure 1, the duty is tracked by uptake via a recognitional arrow (lines 173-180). But earlier the node types include both recognition states and response states, with uptake grouped under recognition (line 129). In Figure 2, there is no separate recognition node; "uptake" receives the recognitional edge (lines 256-265). For this paper's purposes, recognition, uptake, and response should be different nodes: signal/ascription, recognition/classification, status or standing, uptake, response, harm. Otherwise the central claim that recognition can fit, misfit, or create status loses formal discipline.

Line 186 is too strong: "every status that figures in explanation has to be reachable by response states through recognition and its causal uptake." That makes social-status explanation too dependent on downstream recognition. Some social facts may matter because of the frame principles that make them true, even when recognition is absent, delayed, or systematically suppressed. The better claim is narrower: statuses explain projectible social cascades only when recognition and uptake pathways are available in the relevant field.

The discrimination section is the most interesting part of the paper, but it needs one more ontological step. Lines 283-289 distinguish an episode of biased treatment from a stable degraded standing. Good. But the paper should state the threshold question: what makes repeated recognition-response pathways constitute a standing rather than merely cause recurring disadvantage? Ásta helps, but the network itself should say what changes when repeated arrows become a node.

7. **Revision priorities**

1. Define the paper's contribution relative to grounding and anchoring: replacement, supplement, visualization, or explanatory model.
2. Stabilize the recognition/uptake/ascription/status vocabulary and redraw Figure 2 accordingly.
3. Clarify whether normative-transition edges are grounding relations, rule-governed operations, or a distinct dependence type.
4. Split field-validity from successful communal conferral throughout, instead of treating them as one disjunctive assessment.
5. Add one worked comparison showing how the typed-network model explains something that an Epstein-style frame-and-anchor account leaves implicit or obscures.

## Ásta

1. **One-sentence summary** of the paper's main claim

The paper argues that social statuses are best explained by causal-normative networks: typed structures in which normative transitions, constitutive status relations, recognition, and causal uptake jointly explain how statuses can be socially effective, field-valid or successfully conferred, and yet morally unwarranted.

2. **Strengths**

- The paper clearly understands the central non-reductive problem: false promise and discrimination require different treatments, and the draft explicitly says so at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:70).

- The discussion of wrongful conferral is promising. The draft rightly refuses to treat a degraded social standing as mere belief or mere sanction, especially at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:246).

3. **Weaknesses**

- The distinction between **field-validity** and **successful communal conferral** is not yet stable enough. The paper often groups them together, but institutional validity and communal conferral have different success conditions. In the discriminatory communal case, what exactly makes the degraded standing **successfully conferred** rather than merely a stable pattern of wrongful belief, exclusion, and sanction, and who has the standing to confer it if there is no authorizing office?

5. **Verdict**: Revise & Resubmit

The paper has a strong core contribution for Journal of Social Ontology, but the conferralist machinery must be sharpened before submission because the central payoff depends on it.

6. **Detailed comments for the author**

Section 1 sets up the paper well. The contrast between false promise and discrimination at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:68) is exactly the right move. I would make that contrast even more programmatic: false promise is a case of effective misrecognition without the relevant status; discriminatory standing is a case where the status may be socially real because it has been conferred, though it is morally indefensible.

The four-dependence framework is strong, but recognitional dependence needs one more wrinkle. At [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:80), authorized recognition is treated as recognition plus transition. The institutional case is clear. But communal conferral also seems compound: taking someone to have a base property can participate in conferring a status, not merely recognizing one. The paper needs a non-institutional analogue of authorized recognition, or an explanation of why communal conferral is still just a normative-transition edge.

The rule at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:139) says to draw a recognitional edge only where recognition fits an actual status node. Good. But Figure 2 then draws a recognitional edge from "ascribed standing" to "uptake" in the wrongful-conferral case. If the standing is already conferred, that edge is fitting recognition. If the uptake is what confers the standing, the diagram is backwards or at least incomplete. This is the main technical repair I would require.

The discrimination discussion is careful about audit studies. At [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:275), the empirical cases establish patterned uptake, not by themselves conferred standing. The draft says this at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:283), but I would move the warning earlier or make it more explicit: discriminatory treatment becomes a conferred status only when constraints and enablements become durable, mutually reinforcing, and socially navigable as a position.

The statement of conferralism at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:246) should be more exact. "Others take them to have some base property" is too broad. The important issue is not just that others believe or take something to be so; it is that people with standing in that context confer constraints and enablements on that basis. Without "standing in context," the view risks becoming recognition-only construction.

The field section is close, but "field-validity" should probably be reserved for rule-governed institutional domains, while "successful conferral" should be the communal analogue. At [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:316), the contrast between institutional judgment and communal discrimination is helpful. Build the earlier sections around that contrast.

7. **Revision priorities**

1. Redraw or revise Figure 2 so communal conferral is not represented as mere ascription followed by recognition.
2. Define successful communal conferral: who confers, what standing they have, what constraints and enablements result, and how this differs from repeated mistreatment.
3. Separate field-validity from successful conferral more consistently.
4. Specify what "moral warrant" assesses: attribution, conferral, standing, treatment, or all of these under different descriptions.
5. Rework the discrimination section so audit-study evidence is clearly presented as evidence of patterned uptake, not yet evidence of conferred standing by itself.

## Frank Hindriks

The paper argues that social statuses are best explained as positions in typed causal-normative networks that combine causal uptake, normative transitions, recognition, and constitutive ties while separating social efficacy, field-validity or successful conferral, and moral warrant.

What exactly makes a status assignment field-valid or successfully conferred rather than merely recognized, repeated, and causally efficacious, especially in communal cases without offices, formal rules, or explicit authorization?

5. **Verdict**: Revise & Resubmit

The paper has a publishable core for Journal of Social Ontology, but it needs a sharper account of fields, institutional functions, and mechanisms before the typed-network apparatus will look like more than an elegant representational scheme.

6. **Detailed comments for the author**

The opening problem is strong. The Pickwick case gives the paper an intuitive grip on "effective without warrant" ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:62)). But the legal promise example needs institutional care: a social promise, a marriage-promise action, evidentiary finding, verdict, and enforceable legal liability are different status transitions. The draft knows this later, but the opening should already mark the distinction.

The four-edge framework is the main contribution ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:72)). The strongest part is the refusal to model duty and claim-right causally. However, "normative-transition dependence" currently groups together performances, rules, statuses, and transition events ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:78)). An institutionalist will want rules treated differently: rules do not simply generate status changes; they specify the conditions under which acts, offices, records, or procedures count as status-changing.

The "field" definition is too modest for the load it carries. At [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:84), a field is "just a normative practice or domain." But by Section 6 it fixes statuses, authorities, records, defeaters, responses, and projection conditions. That is not "just" a domain; it is the institutional or communal architecture of the network. Define it earlier and more strictly.

The typed-edge model does useful work in the false-promise case ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:135)). Still, the practical node-admission rule is not enough. A reviewer may ask why any explanatory diagram with labels would not qualify. You need constraints: which node types are allowed, what makes a recognitional edge well formed, when one token can occupy two roles, and when authorized recognition requires a distinct institutional rule.

The judgment case is institutionally plausible but compressed. The claim that an erroneous judgment creates "a new procedural and remedial status" is right in spirit ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:207)), but "creates legal liability" can sound too strong ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:211)). Distinguish underlying liability, adjudicated liability, enforceability, preclusion, and remedial powers.

The mechanism section has the right idea but underdelivers for a mechanism-sensitive reader. Modularity and signatures help sort edge types ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:213)), but the paper should give one worked institutional mechanism: office, rule, record, uptake routine, sanction, feedback into expectations. The judgment example is the natural place.

The discrimination section is careful in separating signal manipulation from race itself ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:275)). The weaker step is from repeated biased uptake to conferred standing. "Entrenchment" and "field-licensed" need criteria ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:283)). Who has standing to confer? What licenses the practice if it is wrongful? Is license merely sociological stability, or a practice-internal norm?

Section 6 should be moved earlier or partially previewed earlier. The distinctions between institutional and communal fields at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:314) are central, not housekeeping. They determine whether promise, consent, judgment, and discrimination are structurally analogous or only loosely comparable.

No new source claims are needed for this review. If the revision adds Hindriks-style work on status functions, institutional agency, or social mechanisms, verify the exact sources before citing.

7. **Revision priorities**

1. Define field-validity, successful conferral, enforceability, authority, and moral warrant as separate assessment dimensions.
2. Move the institutional/communal field distinction earlier and use it to organize promise, consent, judgment, and discrimination.
3. Add well-formedness constraints for causal-normative networks so the model is not just a labeled diagram.
4. Develop one full institutional mechanism, preferably judgment: office, rule, record, authority, enforcement, uptake.
5. Clarify the discrimination transition from repeated biased uptake to conferred standing, especially "field-licensed" and "standing to confer."

But those constraints and enablements need to become more concrete. "Exclusion, sanction, and harm" in Figure 2 are too downstream. The degraded standing itself should be cashed out in altered options, credibility, risk, authority, mobility, institutional access, and anticipatory self-regulation.

The caveat at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:250) is important, but it may be too quick. If material and distributive oppression are outside the present account, narrow the abstract and conclusion. If they are inside it, explain how non-recognitional constraints couple to status nodes. Otherwise the paper risks saying "not all oppression," while still letting the central diagram make oppression look like wrongful recognition plus harm.

Figure 2 should probably be revised conceptually, even if not visually. A single "ascription" generating "ascribed standing" makes discriminatory conferral look too much like promising. In oppressive communal cases, the generator is usually a stabilized ascriptive practice: repeated takings, mutual expectations, records, sanctions, and anticipatory adjustments. That should be explicit in the caption or text around [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:273).

The "suppressed entitlement" category at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:240) is excellent and underused. From a non-ideal social ontology perspective, this may be as important as wrongful conferral: oppressed agents often have authority, credibility, claims, or standing that dominant practices refuse to register. Developing this would prevent the paper from centering only statuses imposed by oppressive practices.

The audit-study discussion is careful. The paper correctly says the studies show patterned uptake, not race itself as a manipulable cause ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:275), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:279)). The fields section is helpful, especially the distinction between institutional and communal fields ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:314)). But "agents with standing in that context" needs sharper handling in oppressive cases ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:306)). Oppressors may have social standing to confer a degraded status without having moral authority to do so. That distinction must be unmistakable.

7. **Revision priorities** (top 5)

1. Define moral warrant minimally and apply it to promise, judgment, and discrimination.
2. Rework wrongful conferral so the generator is a stabilized ascriptive practice, not a single ascription.
3. Make constraints and enablements central to the discrimination case, not merely downstream harm.

## Projectibility Reviewer

1. **One-sentence summary** of the paper's projectibility claim

The paper argues that social statuses are projectible when embedded in causal-normative networks: typed, field-relative structures that let us infer what should hold, what will likely be recognized, and what downstream uptake or response will follow, even when social efficacy and moral warrant come apart.

2. **Strengths**

- The draft now foregrounds projectibility rather than hiding it: the abstract says statuses are projectible in causal-normative networks ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:55)), and Sections 2-4 repeatedly frame Khalidi as giving the right demand, namely ordered inference, while causal order alone is too narrow ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:89), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:101), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:184)).
- Mechanisms and edge types are mostly presented as explaining projectibility, not replacing it. The best sentence is: "Projection is what the graph is for" ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:143)).
- The simplified `field` move largely succeeds. `field` is no longer a Boydian HPC; it is a practice/domain that parameterizes projections ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:294), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:310), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:312)).

3. **Weaknesses**

- The introduction still frames the payoff as solving mixed explanation and separating efficacy, validity, conferral, and warrant, rather than as showing why statuses support inductive use. The projectibility claim is present, but the problem statement at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:64) and [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:66) needs to say what the framework lets us predict or infer.
- The conclusion drifts back toward architecture: "edge types explain," "assessment vocabulary says," and "lets the account describe" ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:325)). For this audit, "describe misrecognition" is too weak. The conclusion should cash out: from this status in this field, we can project these defeasible implications and downstream responses.
- Field-relative projectibility is promising but under-cashed. Contract law vs friendship is clear ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:298)); medical ethics and discrimination are present elsewhere but not yet brought under the same field-relative projection test.

4. **Key question** you would ask at Q&A

If the same surface event occurs across contract law, medical ethics, friendship, and discriminatory practice, what exactly does your framework let us project in each field that Khalidi's causal graph or a normative taxonomy alone would not?

5. **Verdict**: Revise & Resubmit

The paper has the right projectibility thesis and the `field` simplification basically works, but the payoff still needs to be stated as "a profile, stabilised by mechanisms, projectible for a purpose," not merely as a better mixed structure.

6. **Detailed comments for the author**

The charitable reading is strong: the paper is not making the common mistake of treating mechanisms as the point. It repeatedly says that order matters because it licenses projection. Lines 89-101 are especially good: Khalidi supplies the standard, promises and consent show why causal order is too narrow, and the paper positions typed edges as an extension rather than a rejection.

The best projectibility passage is [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:184): causal prediction, constitutive read-off, transition projection, and recognitional expectation are distinguished. This is the paper's benchmark sentence. It should control the introduction and conclusion.

The remaining risk is that "typed-edge model" becomes the visible contribution, while projectibility becomes the rationale for the model. That is backwards for a Khalidi/Boyd-facing reader. The model should be presented as the answer to: why is this status/category usable for inference? The slogan should be explicit in substance: a profile, stabilised by mechanisms, projectible for a purpose.

Section 3 does this well. Lines 108-114 explain that normative taxonomy alone cannot predict recognition, apology, enforcement, repair, trust revision, or institutional uptake. This is projectibility as epistemic payoff, not mechanism inventory.

Section 5 is nearly there. The "signatures" are useful, but the section should state the projection each signature licenses. Status-rule signature: change release/withdrawal and project changed permissibility or liability. Recognition signature: change uptake and project demand, callback, sanction, or exclusion without projecting warrant. Authorized recognition: court entry projects procedural liability and enforcement even where the finding is mistaken.

Section 6's discrimination material is good but should sharpen the inductive claim. Lines 283-287 say entrenchment turns repeated uptake into conferred standing and makes recognitional/causal edges "reliably enough to be projectible." That is exactly right, but it needs one more sentence: what can we project once degraded standing exists? Exclusion, lowered credibility, sanction risk, anticipatory self-adjustment, institutional routing, and downstream harm.

The Fields section is the clearest evidence that the recent simplification succeeded. It avoids making `field` a natural kind or extra node ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:302), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:310)). Boyd is used only for the accommodation/projectibility thought ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:298)), which is the right scale.

But field-relative projectibility needs a compact worked contrast. The draft says breach in contract law projects toward remedies and breach in friendship toward resentment and repair. Add medical ethics and discrimination to that same paragraph or immediately after it. The reviewer needs to see that "field" means different licensed projections from the same or similar surface event, not just different background rules.

The conclusion is the main revision target. It should not end with "questions the framework poses" as the final payoff ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Causal-Normative_Networks/main.tex:327)). It should end with what the framework lets us do: distinguish false recognition from wrongful conferral, predict when uptake will matter without warrant, explain why valid judgment and moral defect can coexist, and generalize across promise, consent, judgment, and discrimination without flattening their fields.

No new source claims are introduced in this review.

7. **Revision priorities**

1. Rewrite the introduction's problem statement so the paper asks why statuses are projectible, not only how efficacy without warrant is possible.
2. Add a compact "projection ledger" after the typed-edge model: for each edge type, say what inference it licenses.
3. In Section 5, make each signature end with the projection it supports.
4. Strengthen Section 7 with a four-field contrast: contract law, medical ethics, friendship, discrimination.
5. Rewrite the conclusion around prediction, explanation, and inductive generalization, using the benchmark: "a profile, stabilised by mechanisms, projectible for a purpose."
