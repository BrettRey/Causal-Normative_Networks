## Executive Synthesis

All five reviewers again recommend **Revise and Resubmit**. The board's judgment has improved in kind: no reviewer says the basic distinctions are confused or that the field simplification failed. The paper now has a clear publishable core. The remaining objections are more precise: the paper has to prove that the typed network adds explanatory value beyond grounding/anchoring plus causal mechanisms; define field-validity by criterion rather than list; sharpen recognition and normative-transition typing; and turn the generic projection ledger into a case-indexed ledger.

The most important convergence is that the manuscript now succeeds at separating false recognition from wrongful communal conferral. Reviewers praise the audit-study caution, the episode-versus-position contrast, and the refusal to infer degraded standing directly from signal-response effects. But they also agree that wrongful conferral still needs a more exact account of who confers, in what context, on what base/ascription, with what threshold, and with what constraints and enablements.

The Projectibility Reviewer is the most positive about the revision: projectibility is no longer decorative. The remaining projectibility problem is specificity. The draft says what each edge type licenses in general, but it still lacks field-indexed rows of the form: from node X, in field F, infer Y unless defeater Z, while withholding or assigning warrant status.

## Consensus Strengths

- The forced-choice setup works: causal-only accounts lose normative fit, while normative-taxonomy accounts lose uptake and social cascade.
- The four dependence types are useful and mostly disciplined: causal, constitutive, normative-transition, and recognitional relations are doing real explanatory work.
- The abstract, introduction, Section 2, Section 4 ledger, field section, and conclusion now make projectibility central rather than decorative.
- The judgment case is well chosen because authorized recognition is exactly where institutional power, record, procedure, and enforcement meet.
- The discrimination section is substantially improved: audit studies evidence signal-recognition-response pathways, not race itself as a manipulable cause and not by themselves a degraded standing.
- The distinction between an episode and a position is a major strength and should become the hinge for the wrongful-conferral material.

## Consensus Weaknesses

- **Added value over grounding/anchoring is still not explicit enough.** Epstein's strongest question remains: if frame principles fix grounds, anchors explain why they obtain, and causal mechanisms explain uptake, what does the typed network add?
- **Field-validity remains a list rather than a criterion.** Reviewers want a cleaner distinction among procedural regularity, enforceability, finality, legal effect, institutional validity, communal conferral, truth, and moral warrant.
- **Recognition still carries too many jobs.** The manuscript needs distinct treatment of tracking, misrecognition, authorized recognition, and recognition/uptake as part of communal conferral.
- **Normative-transition is too broad.** Undertaking generating duty, release terminating obligation, rule assigning status, judgment creating procedural position, and uptake helping confer standing may be a family of transition relations rather than one relation type.
- **Wrongful conferral needs conditions.** Agents with standing, context, base/ascription, uptake threshold, durability, and constraints/enablings need to be specified rather than gestured at.
- **Moral warrant is under-analysed.** The paper can avoid a full moral theory, but it needs to distinguish warrant for the status, warrant for conferral, warrant for treatment, correctness of recognition, legitimacy, and truth.
- **Suppressed entitlement is introduced and then dropped.** Either develop it as a real fourth failure profile or remove it from the central taxonomy.

## Productive Tensions

- Epstein presses hardest on whether the network is more than an organizing diagram. The Projectibility Reviewer supplies the best answer: the network earns its keep if it licenses field-indexed projections that grounding/anchoring alone don't display.
- Hindriks wants institutional machinery earlier and sharper; Ásta and Jenkins want communal conferral conditions sharper. These can be handled by making judgment the institutional anchor case and discrimination the communal anchor case.
- Ásta wants Figure 2 to stop making conferral look like mere durable uptake. Jenkins wants material structures not relegated to downstream harm. A revised Figure 2 can distinguish initial recognition/ascription, structurally empowered uptake, successful conferral, and later tracking of the conferred standing.
- The Projectibility Reviewer says the conclusion is now green; the other reviewers still want stronger criteria. The right next step is not another conclusion rewrite but a compact projection/assessment table inside the body.

## Prioritized Revision List

1. **Add an explicit contribution paragraph against the strongest rival:** grounding/anchoring plus causal-mechanistic explanation. State that the network does not replace those stories; it shows the mixed projection profile that they leave distributed across separate explanations.
2. **Add a compact assessment table:** social efficacy, institutional field-validity, communal successful conferral, recognitional fit/truth, enforceability/finality, and moral warrant. Include what each asks, what evidence bears on it, and what it does not imply.
3. **Turn the projection ledger into a real ledger:** rows for promise, consent, judgment, and discrimination; columns for field, source node, edge type, projected target, defeater, and warrant status.
4. **Make judgment the institutional mechanism case:** distinguish underlying liability, finding, adjudicated status, record, preclusion/finality, enforceability/remedial powers, enforcement uptake, and possible error. Verify any added legal distinctions.
5. **Rework wrongful conferral around one specified discrimination example:** base/ascription, perceived property, conferring agents, context, conferred status, constraints/enablings, moral failure, and later recognition of the now-real conferred standing.
6. **Clarify recognition by role and time:** initial recognition of base/ascription, fitting recognition of an actual status, failed recognition with no target, authorized recognition that creates status, and later recognition that tracks a conferred standing.
7. **Either develop suppressed entitlement or cut it from the four-profile taxonomy.** If retained, give one compact case where moral basis is present but institutional or communal recognition fails.

## Verdict

**Revise and Resubmit, with a stronger trajectory than the previous board.** The core is publishable. The next revision should not broaden the literature or add many examples. It should add criteria, tables, and one institutional plus one communal mechanism.

## Full Reviewer Outputs

### Brian Epstein

**One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social statuses are best explained as nodes in typed causal-normative networks that combine causal uptake, normative transitions, recognition, and constitutive ties while separating social efficacy, field-validity or conferral, and moral warrant.

**Strengths**

- The motivating contrast is strong: Pickwick and discrimination let the paper show why “effective” and “warranted” must come apart.
- The four dependence types are clearly introduced and are the right sort of distinctions for a social ontology audience: causal, constitutive, normative-transition, recognitional.
- The paper is admirably explicit that the network does not replace grounding, and that Epstein-style frame principles and anchors still have work to do.
- The “signature” strategy gives the model a real test: change release, recognition, or authorized judgment, and different parts of the structure move.
- The discrimination section is careful not to infer standing directly from audit-study effects; it distinguishes signal-response evidence from an account of degraded social standing.

**Weaknesses**

- The paper hasn’t yet shown that the typed-network apparatus adds explanatory value beyond grounding plus anchoring. The network is described as a representation of grounded statuses, correlatives, recognitions, and uptake. But if anchors already explain why the frame principles are in place, and ordinary causal explanation already handles uptake, what exactly does the network explain that the grounding/anchoring story cannot?
- “Field” is doing too much. It is a normative practice, a parameter, a stabilizing setting, a source of validity conditions, and sometimes a communal conferral context. The paper needs a sharper account of whether fields are anchors, frame-principle packages, explanatory contexts, or socially stabilized practices.
- Recognition is under-specified. The rule says a recognitional edge connects an actual status to recognition that fits it, but discrimination later makes recognition and uptake help confer standing. The paper needs to distinguish recognition as tracking, recognition as causally driving treatment, recognition as authorized status-maker, and recognition as one component in diffuse communal conferral.
- Normative-transition edges risk collapsing into grounding relations without saying so. “Undertaking generates duty,” “rule generates status,” “release terminates obligation,” and “uptake helps confer standing” are not obviously one relation-type. The model needs stricter typing here.
- The opponents are too stylized. “Causal graph with moral labels” and “normative taxonomy with sociology appended” are useful foils, but not yet serious social ontology alternatives. The harder opponent is a grounding/anchoring account supplemented by causal-mechanistic explanation.

**Key Q&A Question**

If frame principles fix the grounding conditions for social statuses and anchors explain why those frame principles obtain, what explanatory work is left for the causal-normative network beyond organizing information already supplied by grounding, anchoring, and ordinary causal explanation?

**Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit**: the paper has a promising core, but it needs to prove that the mixed network is an explanatory model rather than a useful diagram of relations already handled by existing social ontology.

**Revision Priorities**

1. State the added value over Epstein-style grounding and anchoring in one explicit section.
2. Define “field” more tightly and separate field-validity from communal conferral.
3. Rework recognition into distinct subtypes: tracking, misrecognition, authorized recognition, and conferral-involving recognition.
4. Clarify whether normative-transition edges are grounding, powers, rule-governed changes, or a family of relations.
5. Replace the stylized opponents with the strongest alternative: grounding/anchoring plus causal-mechanistic explanation.

Source note: no new external citations introduced. Legal and institutional claims about judgments, preclusion, consent, and resignation should still be checked against authoritative sources before submission.

### Ásta

**One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social statuses are best explained as nodes in typed causal-normative networks that combine normative transitions, constitutive deontic relations, recognitional tracking or misfitting, and causal uptake, thereby explaining how a status can be socially effective or successfully conferred without being morally warranted.

**Strengths**

- The forced-choice framing is strong: the paper clearly shows why neither causal uptake alone nor normative taxonomy alone can handle false promise, consent withdrawal, judgment, and discrimination.
- The four dependence types are useful and mostly well disciplined. The treatment of duty/claim-right as constitutive rather than causal is especially clean.
- The paper correctly resists treating wrongful conferral as mere mistaken belief. The sentence “The ascribed standing is not a mere belief. It is a social position with teeth” is exactly the right conferralist instinct.
- The discrimination section is admirably cautious about audit studies: it says they show a signal-recognition-response pathway, not by themselves a degraded standing. That caution matters.
- The “episode versus position” contrast is one of the paper’s best moves. It gives the author the resources to distinguish repeated discriminatory acts from a conferred social standing.

**Weaknesses**

- The core distinction among efficacy, institutional field-validity, communal successful conferral, and moral warrant is still under-specified. The four questions are introduced, but the paper needs sharper criteria for each. In particular, “field-validity” fits institutional cases, while “successful conferral” fits communal cases; treating them as parallel columns risks making communal conferral look like informal institutional validity.
- The discrimination case still sometimes makes conferral look too much like stabilized uptake. Figure 2 has uptake, records, and expectations generating the ascribed standing, and the prose says durable uptake “can help confer a standing.” But on a conferralist view, the crucial relation is not just repeated treatment; it is agents with standing, in a context, taking someone to have a base property and thereby imposing constraints and enablements. The paper needs to say who confers, in what context, on what basis, and what exact status is conferred.
- The conferred status in the discrimination example is ambiguous. Is the status race, perceived race, racialized standing in hiring, degraded applicant standing, lowered credibility, or subordinated social position? The paper wisely says the audit studies do not establish a degraded standing, but then moves quickly to “degraded standing.” That bridge needs more work.
- Moral warrant is doing too much with too little definition. Moral warrant is “whether the standing or treatment has moral authority,” but standing and treatment can come apart. A conferred status may be real; particular responses to it may be morally forbidden; some institutional mistakes may be procedurally legitimate but substantively false. The paper needs to separate moral authority, truth of the underlying finding, permissibility of enforcement, and justice of the status itself.
- The recognitional edge in wrongful conferral needs temporal indexing. At first, recognition may be of a base property or ascribed property; later, after successful conferral, recognition may track a real conferred standing. Without time indices, Figure 2 risks saying that recognition both creates and fits the same status in the same explanatory moment.

**Key Q&A Question**

In the discrimination case, exactly what is the conferred status, who has standing to confer it, and what distinguishes successful wrongful conferral from stable but merely repeated discriminatory uptake?

**Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The paper has a strong and publishable core, but the conferralist machinery in the discrimination case is not yet stable enough for the paper’s central payoff.

**Revision Priorities**

1. Add a compact diagnostic table distinguishing social efficacy, institutional field-validity, communal successful conferral, moral warrant, and truth or fit of the underlying recognition.
2. Rework the discrimination case around one fully specified example: base property, perceived property, conferring agents, context, conferred status, constraints and enablements, and moral failure.
3. Revise Figure 2 so conferral is not represented as mere uptake becoming durable. Include agents-with-standing or otherwise mark the conferral relation more explicitly.
4. Clarify moral warrant: distinguish warrant for the status, warrant for the conferral act, warrant for downstream treatment, and correctness of recognition.
5. Add temporal indexing to wrongful conferral: initial recognition or misrecognition, uptake, successful conferral, later recognition of the now-real conferred standing.

### Frank Hindriks

**One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social statuses are projectible only when represented in typed causal-normative networks that distinguish causal uptake, normative transitions, constitutive relations, and recognition, thereby explaining how a status can be socially effective, institutionally or communally real, and yet morally unwarranted.

**Strengths**

- The paper has a clear and attractive target: it rejects both causal reduction and purely normative taxonomy, and it shows why promise, consent, judgment, and discrimination need a mixed explanatory structure.
- The four dependence types are promising and mostly well-motivated: causal, constitutive, normative-transition, and recognitional relations do mark different explanatory roles. The discussion of duty/claim-right correlativity is especially helpful.
- The distinction between social efficacy, field-validity, successful communal conferral, and moral warrant is the paper’s strongest contribution. It lets the author say, correctly, that a status-assignment may have real social effects without moral authority.
- The judgment case is well chosen. The paper sees that a court judgment is not merely evidence and not merely a cause of enforcement, but an authorized act that changes procedural status. That is exactly the sort of case a social ontology journal will care about.
- The discrimination section is careful not to infer degraded standing directly from audit-study evidence. The paper distinguishes signal-recognition-response pathways from the stronger claim that a standing has been conferred.

**Weaknesses**

- Field-validity is still underdefined. The paper says field-validity concerns “right party, office, procedure, form, record, rule, or authority,” but this is a list rather than a criterion. A JSO reviewer will ask: valid in what sense? Procedurally regular, legally effective, final, enforceable, non-void, non-defective, accepted by the practice? The judgment case especially needs sharper distinctions between underlying liability, adjudicated liability, enforceability, finality, and moral warrant. If legal distinctions such as void/voidable judgment or res judicata are added, they need verification.
- The promise and consent cases are institutionally too quick. The paper repeatedly says an undertaking generates a duty and consent generates permission, but it does not yet specify the relevant rules, standing conditions, uptake conditions, competence conditions, scope conditions, or defects. Ordinary promising, contract law, medical consent, and moral consent are different fields. The paper’s own field-relative framework requires those differences to be made explicit.
- The typed network risks becoming a disciplined diagram rather than a theory. The admission rule is “add a node only if leaving it out would make the explanation worse.” That is pragmatically sensible, but too loose for the paper’s central apparatus. The reader needs stronger well-formedness constraints: which kinds of nodes may stand in which edge relations, when a status node is licensed, when a recognition node is merely a belief, and what counts as stabilization rather than repeated response.
- Recognition is doing two different jobs. In some places recognition is an epistemic or representational fit relation from status to recognition; in others, authorized recognition helps create a status. The paper says authorized recognition is compound rather than primitive, which is plausible, but the basic recognitional edge still needs clarification. Is it a dependence relation, an accuracy relation, an evidential relation, or a norm of fit?
- Institutions, functions, and mechanisms enter too late and too thinly. Searle’s status functions appear only in the fields section, after the network model is already built. But institutional functions, offices, records, and enforcement mechanisms are not decorative downstream items; they are what make judgments, registrations, contracts, and official classifications institutionally powerful. The model needs to show where functions and mechanisms sit in the network, not merely say that fields parameterize them.

**Key Q&A Question**

What exactly makes an assignment field-valid rather than merely effective, recognized, enforceable, or successfully conferred, and can you state that criterion in a way that handles promises, medical consent, court judgments, and discriminatory standing without changing its meaning across cases?

**Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The core idea is strong and suitable for JSO, but the paper needs sharper institutional machinery before the typed network can carry the explanatory weight placed on it.

**Revision Priorities**

1. Define field-validity explicitly, distinguishing it from efficacy, enforceability, finality, communal conferral, and moral warrant.
2. Rework the judgment case as the institutional anchor case: separate underlying status, finding, adjudicated status, procedural consequence, enforcement, and possible error.
3. Specify field conditions for promise and consent: standing, authority, competence, scope, defects, withdrawal, and field-relative differences between ordinary, moral, legal, and medical contexts.
4. Add well-formedness constraints for the graph: permissible node types, edge types, edge direction, absent nodes, failed recognition, and compound authorized-recognition cases.
5. Bring institutional functions and social mechanisms into the model earlier, showing how offices, records, procedures, and enforcement routines stabilize the projections rather than merely accompanying them.

### Katharine Jenkins

**One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social statuses are best explained through typed causal-normative networks that distinguish causal uptake, normative transitions, constitutive ties, and recognition, thereby explaining how a status can be socially effective, institutionally or communally real, and still lack moral warrant.

**Strengths**

- The paper clearly understands the false dilemma it targets: reducing status either to causal treatment or to normatively valid standing. The contrast is crisp and productive.
- The four dependence types are useful and disciplined. The distinction between causal uptake, normative transition, constitutive correlation, and recognition is not cosmetic; it does real explanatory work in the promise, consent, judgment, and discrimination cases.
- The paper avoids a common mistake about discrimination: it explicitly says audit studies show signal-recognition-response pathways, not race itself as an ordinary manipulable cause and not yet a degraded standing.
- The distinction between false recognition and wrongful communal conferral is especially promising. This is exactly where the paper can contribute to non-ideal social ontology.
- The restriction that not all oppression is recognitional or status-conferral-based is important and shows admirable caution.

**Weaknesses**

- The account of moral warrant is too thin for the work it is doing. Moral warrant is defined as “whether the standing or treatment has moral authority rather than imposing unjust constraint,” but the paper never says what moral authority amounts to, what makes a constraint unjust, or how warrant differs from legitimacy, permissibility, validity, and non-oppressiveness. The phrase “moral-warrant column remains empty” risks becoming a label for everything normatively bad rather than an analysed distinction.
- Wrongful conferral still risks reducing oppression to misrecognition-plus-entrenchment. The caveat says material, distributive, and coercive structures can enter as causal constraints, resources, built environments, or enforcement routines, but they remain peripheral. If oppression is partly constituted by material constraint, not merely caused downstream by conferred standing, Figure 2 may underdescribe the phenomenon.
- “Agents with social standing” is doing too much work. It is not clear who has standing to confer degraded statuses in oppressive contexts: dominant-group members, institutions, gatekeepers, the wider community, or only those whose uptake is structurally empowered. Without this, the account may count too many stable patterns of mistreatment as conferred statuses.
- The threshold from episode to standing is under-specified. The contrast between “a single employer’s biased callback decision” and a stable degraded standing is right, but the paper needs criteria: repetition by whom, across which sites, with what durability, and with what constraints or enablements? Otherwise “entrenchment” names the thing to be explained.
- Suppressed entitlement is introduced as a fourth failure profile but then drops out. From a non-ideal social ontology perspective, this may be as important as wrongful conferral: oppressed people often have claims, powers, credibility, or authority that institutions refuse to register. The paper should either develop this case or remove it from the central taxonomy.

**Key Q&A Question**

When a discriminatory standing is “successfully conferred” but morally unwarranted, what exactly makes it a real social status rather than a stable pattern of harmful treatment, and whose standing or uptake has the authority to confer it?

**Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The core ontology is promising and disciplined, but the oppression and wrongful-conferral claims need a stronger account of moral warrant, social standing, and material constraint before the paper is ready for JSO.

**Revision Priorities**

1. Define moral warrant more substantively, or explicitly limit the paper to separating warrant from efficacy without giving a full moral theory.
2. Specify the conditions under which repeated discriminatory uptake becomes a conferred standing rather than merely repeated wrongful treatment.
3. Clarify who has social standing to confer communal statuses in oppressive contexts, especially where counter-practices and resistance coexist.
4. Rework the discrimination section so constraints and enablements are not just listed but explain the content of the degraded standing.
5. Develop suppressed entitlement as a non-ideal case, or cut it from the four-part taxonomy.

### Projectibility Reviewer

**One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that social statuses are projectible only when represented in typed causal-normative networks that distinguish causal uptake, normative transition, recognition, and constitutive dependence while separating social efficacy, field-validity, communal conferral, and moral warrant.

**Strengths**

- The paper frames projectibility as central from the abstract onward: the model “explains which inferences a status supports in a field” rather than merely redescribing social mechanisms.
- Section 2 preserves Khalidi’s core point that projectibility requires ordered dependence, then argues that moral and institutional order is not always causal order.
- The typed-edge account has a genuinely projective payoff: different edge types license different inferences, especially causal predictions, constitutive read-offs, normative-transition projections, and recognitional expectations.
- The discrimination section avoids the common reduction of discrimination to signal-response causality by saying audit studies evidence uptake but do not by themselves establish degraded standing.
- The conclusion does cash out usable projections: release projects lapse of obligation, judgment projects procedural consequences, discriminatory standing projects credibility loss, constrained options, sanction risk, routing, and self-adjustment.

**Weaknesses**

- The “projection ledger” is still more a taxonomy of inference types than a ledger. Line 186 says what each edge type licenses, but it does not yet give enough case-specific entries of the form: from node X, under field F, infer Y unless defeater Z. This is the main projectibility gap.
- The field section preserves Boyd-adjacent field relativity, but “stable enough to support projection” remains doing too much work. The paper needs criteria for when a practice is projectibility-conferring rather than merely sociologically persistent.
- Mechanisms and practices sometimes threaten to become the point after all. Records, registers, routines, sanction, training, and uptake stabilize the field, but the paper should say more explicitly what each stabilizer lets us infer and when the inference fails.
- Section 6 gives the best substantive case, but the move from repeated uptake to conferred standing is still under-specified. The paper says entrenchment under agents with standing turns biased uptake into standing, but it needs sharper thresholds or diagnostics for when repeated arrows become a stable status node.
- The abstract and introduction are good, but they could be even more projectibility-forward. The current first sentence emphasizes efficacy without warrant; the contribution would be clearer if the abstract named the distinctive projections the account makes unavailable to causal-only or normative-only rivals.

**Key Q&A Question**

What exact projection becomes licensed when a discriminatory practice crosses from repeated causal uptake into conferred degraded standing, and what evidence would show that this projection is unavailable from the signal-recognition-response pathway alone?

**Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit**: projectibility is doing real structural work, but the paper still needs a more explicit, field-indexed projection ledger before the network account fully earns its advertised payoff.

**Revision Priorities**

1. Add an explicit projection ledger, preferably in Section 4, with rows for promise, consent, judgment, and discrimination; columns should include edge type, field, source node, projected target, defeater, and warrant status.
2. Define “stable enough to support projection” in Section 7 with usable diagnostics, not just examples.
3. In Section 6, specify when repeated discriminatory uptake becomes conferred standing rather than merely recurring harm.
4. Retune the abstract and opening problem so the paper’s contribution is stated as “what the network lets us infer, predict, explain, or generalize” before the apparatus is introduced.
5. Add one paragraph after line 186 showing how a single case yields different projections by edge type and by field.
